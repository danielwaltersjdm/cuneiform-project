# Corpus Survey: Search Specifications

_Revised: 2026-04-02_

---

## Purpose

Identify and document every known ancient documentary corpus that could plausibly contain evidence relevant to behavioral economics research: overconfidence, loss aversion, prospect theory, reference dependence, and related judgment biases. This document will serve as a citable record of the full landscape considered, and will justify any later decisions about what to analyze.

---

## Guiding Principle

**No corpus is excluded at the survey stage.**

Exclusion decisions belong in the analysis stage, not here. The survey is a map of what exists. Premature exclusion risks missing something valuable, and storage is cheap. A corpus we download and don't use costs us nothing. A corpus we didn't survey and later need costs us a return trip.

The three stages are kept deliberately separate:

| Stage | Question | Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Survey (this document)** | Does it exist? | None — document everything |
| **2. Download** | Is it accessible? | Practical only: digital availability, format, size |
| **3. Translate / Analyze** | Is it worth processing? | Relevance, cost, feasibility |

---

## Temporal Scope

**Lower bound:** c. 3000 BCE — earliest legible administrative and economic writing (Late Uruk cuneiform). Earlier marks exist (~3200 BCE) but are not yet interpretable for content.

**Upper bound: 640 CE** — the Arab conquest of Egypt and the Levant.

Rationale for this cutoff:
- Ends the continuous papyrus documentary tradition in Egypt
- Cuneiform had already died out by ~75 CE
- Captures all major ancient Mediterranean and Near Eastern commercial traditions in their pre-institutional form — no central banks, no formalized insurance, no limited liability — the environment where behavioral biases are most visible and least corrected by market structure
- After 640 CE, Islamic, Byzantine, and early medieval European commerce belongs to separate historiographical traditions with their own established methods

Corpora that straddle the cutoff are included in full.

---

## Geographic Scope

All literate cultures producing documentary writing in this period, including but not limited to:

- Mesopotamia (modern Iraq, Syria, southeast Turkey)
- Egypt (Nile Valley and Delta)
- The Levant (modern Israel, Palestine, Jordan, Lebanon, coastal Syria)
- Anatolia (modern Turkey)
- Iran and Persia
- The Hellenistic and Roman Mediterranean world
- Arabia (pre-Islamic, where documentary evidence exists)
- Any other region with surviving economic or administrative writing

**On East Asia:** Chinese oracle bones will be assessed on their own merits. The divination record structure — question posed, outcome later verified — is directly relevant to overconfidence and forecast calibration research, regardless of the cultural distance from the Mediterranean corpora.

**On other regions:** Sub-Saharan Africa, the Americas, and South/East Asia are not actively searched, not because they lack interest, but because surviving pre-640 CE documentary traditions in those regions are either non-existent, undeciphered, or would constitute a separate project. If evidence is encountered incidentally it will be noted.

---

## Writing Systems and Languages

The survey covers all ancient writing systems with surviving documentary corpora in the temporal and geographic scope above:

Cuneiform (Sumerian, all Akkadian dialects), Egyptian hieroglyphics, hieratic, demotic, Coptic, Greek (Linear B through Byzantine), Aramaic, Hebrew, Phoenician and Punic, Ugaritic, Latin, and others as encountered.

---

## What the Survey Records for Each Corpus

For every corpus identified, the survey will document:

- **Name and common abbreviations**
- **Date range**
- **Language and script**
- **Provenance** (where the physical documents were found)
- **Approximate size** (number of documents, if known)
- **Document types** (letters, administrative records, legal contracts, literary, religious, etc.)
- **Translation status** (fully translated / partially / untranslated)
- **Digital availability** (freely downloadable / behind paywall / physical archives only)
- **Primary digital home** (CDLI, ORACC, Papyri.info, etc.)
- **Behavioral economics signal** (a plain-language note on what kinds of bias evidence might be present)
- **Overall assessment** (a brief qualitative note — not an exclusion decision)

## Search Sources

1. **CDLI** — Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative
2. **ORACC** — Open Richly Annotated Cuneiform Corpus
3. **Papyri.info** — Greek and Demotic papyri (DDbDP + HGV + APIS)
4. **Thesaurus Linguae Aegyptiae (TLA)** — Egyptian texts
5. **ARCHIBAB** — Old Babylonian archives
6. **BDTNS** — Database of Neo-Sumerian Texts
7. **Published corpus catalogues and review articles** (via web search)
8. Expert knowledge, cross-checked against the above

---

## Output

The survey produces a single file: `docs/corpus_survey.md`

One entry per corpus. No ranked tiers, no exclusions. Format is a structured reference document — the raw material for later decisions, not the decisions themselves.
