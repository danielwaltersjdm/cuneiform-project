**Prepared:** April 2026
**Project:** Behavioral Biases in Ancient Economic Decision-Making
**Time Window:** c. 3000 BCE – 640 CE
**Geographic Scope:** Mesopotamia, Egypt, Levant, Anatolia, Persia, Hellenistic and Roman Mediterranean; Central Asia and Chinese oracle bones assessed on merit

## Introduction

This document surveys every major ancient documentary corpus that may contain evidence bearing on behavioral economics research — specifically on overconfidence, loss aversion, prospect theory, reference dependence, status quo bias, and related judgment biases. The goal is comprehensive cataloguing for citation in subsequent publications: no corpus is excluded at this stage, though each is rated for behavioral-signal strength. Researchers should treat relevance ratings as provisional; the underlying signal quality depends heavily on how texts are sampled and which agents can be tracked across multiple documents.

The corpora span roughly five thousand years and multiple writing systems (cuneiform, hieroglyphic/hieratic, alphabetic, Linear syllabic, and Greek/Latin). Behavioral signals are strongest where three conditions co-occur: (1) named, recurring economic agents whose decisions can be traced across time; (2) transaction data recording prices, quantities, exchange rates, or interest; and (3) documents that record either expectations/forecasts or post-hoc complaints and disputes. The Old Assyrian merchant archive at Kanesh, the Babylonian Astronomical Diaries, and the Roman Egypt papyri from Oxyrhynchus score highest on all three dimensions. Many other corpora, however, offer partial evidence — especially recurring-agent data and loan/debt documents — that may prove valuable when combined with price series from other sources.

## 1. Mesopotamian Cuneiform Corpora

### 1.1 Late Uruk Administrative Tablets (c. 3350–2900 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Proto-cuneiform, Uruk IV–III
**Date range:** c. 3350–2900 BCE
**Language and script:** Proto-cuneiform (undeciphered logographic predecessor to Sumerian cuneiform)
**Provenance:** Excavated at Uruk (modern Warka), Iraq; scattered pieces at Jemdet Nasr, Tell Uqair, and Khafajah
**Approximate size:** ~5,000 tablets and fragments from Uruk IV and III phases (less than 6,000 total known)
**Document types:** Administrative accounts (rations, commodities, labor), lexical lists, numerical records
**Translation status:** Minimally translated; the script remains largely undeciphered. Numerical notations are understood; logograms can often be identified. No running prose.
**Digital availability:** Freely available via CDLI (https://cdli.earth); the entire archaic corpus in transliteration is online with many images.
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth (search: "Uruk IV" or "Uruk III" period)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Transaction records: commodity quantities, ration allocations, and labor counts are explicit; price equivalencies are implicit
- Numerical notation systems allow ratio analysis (reference-price benchmarking)
- No named individuals recoverable; no letter or complaint genre
- Conditional instructions: absent
- Repeated agents: not identifiable

**Assessment:** The earliest surviving administrative writing offers counts and allocations but no individual decision-makers. The corpus establishes quantitative benchmarks for the period but cannot support individual-level behavioral analysis. Low behavioral signal for bias detection; moderate value for establishing ancient price/ration reference points.

### 1.2 Early Dynastic Lagash/Girsu Archives (c. 2600–2350 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** ED Lagash; Girsu texts
**Date range:** c. 2600–2350 BCE (Early Dynastic IIIa–IIIb)
**Language and script:** Sumerian; proto-cuneiform/early cuneiform
**Provenance:** Girsu (modern Telloh) and Lagash, southern Iraq; excavated largely by French missions in the 19th century
**Approximate size:** ~1,800 administrative tablets from the reigns of the last three rulers of Lagash; several thousand more from the broader ED period at Girsu
**Document types:** Temple administrative records, ration lists, land grants, royal inscriptions
**Translation status:** Partially translated; major collections published in French (Thureau-Dangin) and English; ongoing digitization
**Digital availability:** Partially available via CDLI (https://cdli.earth); images and transliterations for many tablets
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Ration and commodity records (implicit price benchmarks)
- Temple institutional records (reference-point allocations)
- No individual merchant correspondence
- Conditional instructions: absent at this date
- Repeated agents: temple officials appear in rosters but linking across documents is difficult

**Assessment:** An important baseline corpus for institutional allocation behavior in early city-states. Temple records show hierarchical distribution of rations that could be analyzed for reference-point effects. Individual decision-maker data are largely absent. Low-to-medium behavioral signal.

### 1.3 Ur III Administrative Archives (c. 2112–2004 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Ur III; Neo-Sumerian; BDTNS; Drehem/Puzrish-Dagan
**Date range:** c. 2112–2004 BCE
**Language and script:** Sumerian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Multiple sites — Puzrish-Dagan (modern Drehem), Girsu, Umma, Nippur, Lagash, and many others; tablets dispersed across >200 museums worldwide
**Approximate size:** ~120,000 tablets known (105,000+ catalogued in BDTNS as of 2024); the Oriental Institute holds ~1,100 Drehem tablets alone
**Document types:** Administrative accounts (cattle, grain, textiles, silver), receipts, ration lists, messenger texts, labor records; very few letters
**Translation status:** Partially translated at scale; most tablets have transliterations in CDLI/BDTNS but full English translations exist only for selected sub-corpora
**Digital availability:** Freely available — CDLI (https://cdli.earth) and BDTNS (https://bdtns.filol.csic.es) together offer the largest open-access cuneiform corpus
**Primary digital home:** https://bdtns.filol.csic.es; https://cdli.earth

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Transaction records: prices in silver, grain equivalencies, commodity exchange rates — very extensive
- Named officials appear across hundreds of texts (potential repeated-agent tracking)
- Delivery and receipt records enable expectation-vs-outcome comparison at institutional level
- Loan documents: silver and grain loans documented
- Conditional clauses: appear in some contracts
- Complaint/grievance: rare but present in messenger texts
- The Drehem livestock redistribution archive is particularly rich for quantity/price analysis

**Assessment:** The single largest uniform administrative corpus from the ancient world. The sheer volume and systematic nature of Ur III bookkeeping makes it the best candidate for large-scale quantitative analysis of institutional reference pricing and commodity flows. Individual agency is limited (texts record transactions, not reasoning), but named officials create potential for repeated-agent networks. High relevance for price-series and institutional-behavior analysis; medium relevance for individual bias detection.

### 1.4 Ebla Archives (c. 2400–2300 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Ebla; ARET (Archivi Reali di Ebla — Testi); EbDA
**Date range:** c. 2400–2300 BCE (destroyed c. 2300 BCE)
**Language and script:** Eblaite (early Semitic) and Sumerian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Tell Mardikh, northwestern Syria; excavated by Italian Archaeological Mission (MAIS) beginning 1974
**Approximate size:** ~17,000 tablets and fragments recovered; the Ebla Digital Archives project targets ~3,000 tablets published in ARET
**Document types:** Palace administrative texts, lexical lists, diplomatic correspondence, commercial records (metal, textile, and grain distributions)
**Translation status:** Partially translated; ARET series publishes editions with Italian commentary; English translations available for selected texts; full English translations ongoing
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Ebla Digital Archives (http://ebda.cnr.it/) provides digital edition of published tablets; CDLI has records for many
**Primary digital home:** http://ebda.cnr.it/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Commercial distribution records (metals, textiles, grain) with quantities
- Diplomatic exchange records (gift equivalencies as price signals)
- Palace messenger and reporting texts
- Named officials in distribution records
- No merchant letters; no complaint genre

**Assessment:** A major Bronze Age palace archive with substantial commercial records. The Ebla texts document gift-exchange diplomacy and internal distribution that could be analyzed for reference-pricing. Translation access is primarily through specialized Italian scholarship. Medium behavioral signal for institutional behavior; low for individual bias.

### 1.5 Old Assyrian / Kanesh Merchant Texts (c. 1950–1750 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** OA; Kārum Kanesh; CCT; AKT; OATP
**Date range:** c. 1950–1750 BCE (Level II: c. 1950–1836 BCE; Level Ib: c. 1798–1750 BCE)
**Language and script:** Old Assyrian dialect of Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Kültepe (ancient Kanesh), central Anatolia (Turkey); excavated by Turkish missions since 1948
**Approximate size:** ~23,000 tablets total excavated; ~1,000 letters in CDLI; the full corpus of published texts runs to many thousands
**Document types:** Private merchant letters, contracts (partnership agreements, loans, consignment), legal documents, accounts, lists — essentially the complete business records of private trader families
**Translation status:** Partially translated — key archives (Pūšu-kēn, Imdīlum, Šū-Hubur families) translated in scholarly editions; major reference works by Veenhof, Michel, Dercksen; a substantial portion remains in Turkish museum archives unpublished
**Digital availability:** Partially available — CDLI has transliterations for >1,000 letters; Wikisource and academic repositories have scattered editions; no single comprehensive free digital corpus exists
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth (search: "Old Assyrian"); UNESCO memory document: https://media.unesco.org/sites/default/files/webform/mow001/turkey_kultepe_eng.pdf

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Highest-signal corpus for individual behavioral economics**: named traders traceable across hundreds of letters
- Private merchant letters document risk-taking, optimistic forecasts, complaints about prices, and loss grievances explicitly
- Price information: commodity prices (textiles, tin, silver equivalencies), transport costs, profit expectations
- Forecast records: letters explicitly state expected vs. realized prices ("I expected 15 shekels but got only 9")
- Complaint letters: abundant — loss framing ("I have lost my investment"), grievance about unfair dealing
- Loan/debt documents: interest-bearing loans (typically 30% per year), debt restructuring
- Conditional instructions: ubiquitous ("If prices are good, sell; if not, store")
- Partnership contracts with asymmetric terms (potential reference-dependence evidence)
- Named repeated agents: thousands of transactions traceable to ~50 core family networks
- Women (wives at Ashur) acted as independent merchants — provides within-gender comparison
- Social-network analysis has been applied (Barjamovic et al. 2019)

**Assessment:** The single most important corpus for individual-level behavioral economics in antiquity. The Old Assyrian letters are private business correspondence from identifiable people who made repeated decisions under uncertainty, recorded their expectations, and reported outcomes. Loss aversion, overconfidence, and reference dependence can all be operationalized directly. This corpus is already partially downloaded for this project. **Highest behavioral economics relevance.**

### 1.6 Mari Archives (c. 1800–1760 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** ARM (Archives royales de Mari); LAPO; FM (Florilegium Marianum)
**Date range:** c. 1800–1760 BCE (reign of Zimri-Lim and predecessors)
**Language and script:** Old Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Tell Hariri (ancient Mari), on the Euphrates, eastern Syria; excavated by French missions since 1933
**Approximate size:** ~20,000 tablets total found in the palace; ~3,000 published (including ~1,077 letters, ~1,760 economic/administrative/juridical texts)
**Document types:** Royal and administrative correspondence, economic accounts, juridical records, prophetic texts, administrative ration lists
**Translation status:** Partially translated — published texts available in ARM series (French) and LAPO (French/English editions); major collections translated; ~17,000 tablets remain unpublished
**Digital availability:** Minimally available digitally — EHKM (Elektronisches Handbuch der Keilschrifttexte aus Mari) is a bibliographic database; CDLI has records; no comprehensive free online edition of texts
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth; http://archibab.fr (some coverage)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Royal correspondence documents decision-making under uncertainty (military, trade, diplomacy)
- Economic records: commodity distribution, silver and grain accounts
- Administrative letters: reports to the king on market prices, supply shortfalls
- Prophetic texts: explicit forecasts and conditional predictions ("If you do X, the god says Y will happen")
- Named officials across time: traceable royal officials
- Complaint reports: supply failures and disruptions documented
- Loan documents: appear in juridical texts

**Assessment:** A major Old Babylonian royal archive with important economic correspondence. The prophetic texts are particularly interesting as documented forecast records. The low publication rate (3,000 of 20,000 tablets) limits current research scope. Medium-to-high behavioral signal once published texts are systematically analyzed; the prophetic corpus offers unique forecast-outcome pairing potential.

### 1.7 Old Babylonian Period: Sippar, Larsa, and Private Archives (c. 2000–1600 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** OB; ARCHIBAB; AbB (Altbabylonische Briefe)
**Date range:** c. 2000–1600 BCE
**Language and script:** Old Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Sippar (Abu Habba), Larsa, Nippur, Isin, Ur, and many other southern Mesopotamian sites; tablets dispersed worldwide
**Approximate size:** Tens of thousands of tablets — ARCHIBAB database (archibab.fr) contains lemmatized editions of a large sub-corpus; AbB series covers ~3,000 letters; the British Museum holds thousands of Sippar tablets
**Document types:** Commercial letters, contracts (sale, lease, hire, loan), legal records, temple administrative records, school texts, mathematical tablets
**Translation status:** Partially translated — AbB series provides ~3,000 letter translations; ARCHIBAB (archibab.fr) provides a growing lemmatized corpus with translations; many thousands remain untranslated in museum collections
**Digital availability:** Partially available — ARCHIBAB (https://archibab.fr) is the primary portal for OB texts online; CDLI has records and images
**Primary digital home:** https://archibab.fr; https://cdli.earth

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Loan documents**: extensive silver and grain loan contracts with interest rates (typically 20–33% per annum), enabling risk preference analysis
- Price series: commodity prices (silver, barley, sesame, wool) documented across decades in merchant archives
- Private merchant letters: smaller in scale than Kanesh but similar in character (named traders, investment decisions, complaints)
- Conditional instructions in contracts: standard formulae ("if the debtor fails to pay…")
- Court records: dispute resolution with explicit claim and counterclaim framing
- Named individuals: some recurring across multiple documents in the same city archive
- Temple archives (Sippar Shamash temple) show institutional pricing decisions

**Assessment:** A rich and extensive set of corpora spanning a long period. The loan document archive is particularly valuable for risk preference analysis. ARCHIBAB provides the best current digital access. The Old Babylonian period is widely considered the most developed ancient market economy, with sophisticated financial instruments. **High behavioral relevance**, especially for loan/interest analysis and price series.

### 1.8 Middle Babylonian / Kassite Archives (c. 1600–1155 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** MB; Kassite; Nippur Kassite
**Date range:** c. 1600–1155 BCE
**Language and script:** Middle Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Nippur (primary site, 90% of Kassite tablets), Ur, Babylon, and other sites; Iraq
**Approximate size:** ~12,000 tablets from the Kassite period; fewer than 10% published
**Document types:** Administrative records, letters, contracts, ritual texts, lexical lists
**Translation status:** Minimally translated — fewer than 10% have been published in editions; most remain in museum collections unedited
**Digital availability:** Minimally available — CDLI holds records and some transliterations; ISAC (Chicago) published a catalogue
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative records with commodity data
- Some loan contracts
- Diplomatic correspondence (Amarna-period Akkadian letters overlap this period)
- Named administrative officials across some documents

**Assessment:** An understudied corpus with significant gaps. The low publication rate severely limits current behavioral analysis. The Nippur concentration suggests potential for systematic archive study once more texts are published. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** currently; higher potential once publication catches up.

### 1.9 Nuzi Archives (c. 1500–1350 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Nuzi; HSS (Harvard Semitic Series); AASOR
**Date range:** c. 1450–1350 BCE (five generations of archive)
**Language and script:** Hurrian-influenced Akkadian (Nuzi dialect); cuneiform
**Provenance:** Nuzi (modern Yorghan Tepe), near Kirkuk, Iraq; excavated by American School of Oriental Research 1925–1933
**Approximate size:** ~5,000 tablets; held primarily at Oriental Institute (Chicago), Harvard Semitic Museum, and Iraq Museum
**Document types:** Land transaction contracts, marriage documents, wills, court records, loan tablets, slavery contracts, administrative texts; ~one quarter concern a single family (Tehip-tilla)
**Translation status:** Partially translated — HSS series publishes major sub-corpora; the Tehip-tilla family dossier (~500 texts) is especially well studied; ongoing work by Brigitte Lion and others
**Digital availability:** Partially available — CDLI has records; some sub-corpora accessible via academic repositories; no single comprehensive free portal
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Land transaction contracts with price data (enables reference-point analysis across a community)
- **Repeated-agent analysis**: Tehip-tilla son of Puhi-shenni appears in >500 documents — possibly the best-documented individual economic agent before modern times
- Loan contracts: silver and grain loans with interest
- Court records: dispute framing, loss claims
- Marriage and inheritance documents: endowment reference points
- Conditional clauses: standard in contracts

**Assessment:** The Tehip-tilla family dossier is a remarkable resource: a single agent's transactions over two generations, enabling direct analysis of how one person's reference prices and risk preferences evolved. The broader Nuzi corpus is rich in private contracts. **High behavioral relevance** for individual-level analysis, particularly for reference dependence and loss framing in land transactions.

### 1.10 Ugarit Economic and Administrative Archives (c. 1400–1185 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** RS (Ras Shamra); KTU (Keilalphabetische Texte aus Ugarit); PRU (Palais Royal d'Ugarit)
**Date range:** c. 1400–1185 BCE (destroyed c. 1185 BCE)
**Language and script:** Ugaritic (alphabetic cuneiform), Akkadian (syllabic cuneiform), and Hurrian; multiple scripts
**Provenance:** Ras Shamra (ancient Ugarit), coastal Syria; excavated by French missions since 1929
**Approximate size:** ~2,000 tablets total; ~872 administrative/economic in Ugaritic (KTU 4); hundreds in Akkadian
**Document types:** Administrative records (rations, tax lists, inventories), legal contracts, diplomatic letters, merchant correspondence, literary texts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — KTU provides standard transliterations; major economic texts translated in PRU series and specialized studies; literary texts well translated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — CDLI has Ugaritic records; KTU transliterations available in academic software (Logos); no comprehensive free online edition of economic texts
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth; https://www.cdli.ox.ac.uk/wiki/doku.php?id=ugaritic

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Tax registers and ration lists (institutional reference pricing)
- Legal contracts with commodity price data
- Some merchant correspondence
- Diplomatic exchange records (gift equivalencies)
- Named individuals in administrative rosters
- The archive was destroyed suddenly (c. 1185 BCE) — the last tablets may reflect crisis decision-making

**Assessment:** A valuable Late Bronze Age archive with good economic content. The Ugaritic administrative texts are a well-defined corpus. The bilingual nature (Ugaritic and Akkadian) adds complexity but also richness. Medium behavioral signal; the tax and distribution records are particularly useful for institutional pricing analysis.

### 1.11 Middle Assyrian Archives (c. 1400–1050 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** MA; ATMAP (Archival Texts of the Middle Assyrian Period); TCMA
**Date range:** c. 1400–1050 BCE
**Language and script:** Middle Assyrian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Assur (modern Qal'at Sharqat), Iraq; also Dur-Katlimmu, Tell Sabi Abyad, and other sites
**Approximate size:** ~3,000 tablets from various sites
**Document types:** Administrative texts, legal records, royal decrees, administrative letters, harem texts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — ATMAP (Oracc) provides transliterations and translations for published administrative documents; palace decrees well translated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — ATMAP project on Oracc (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/tcma/)
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/tcma/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative records with commodity data
- Royal decrees with conditional clauses
- Legal records with price data
- Palace administrative distribution records
- Harem administrative texts (ration allocation, reference-point analysis possible)

**Assessment:** A moderately sized corpus with administrative and legal content. Less commercially oriented than OA or OB corpora but contains useful institutional data. Medium-low behavioral signal; value primarily in ration/allocation data for reference-point analysis.

### 1.12 Neo-Assyrian State Archives (SAA Series, c. 911–609 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** SAA (State Archives of Assyria); SAAo (online); NATCP
**Date range:** c. 911–609 BCE
**Language and script:** Neo-Assyrian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Nineveh (Kuyunjik), Kalhu (Nimrud), and other Assyrian sites; Iraq
**Approximate size:** 5,056 texts published in the SAA series (21 of 22 planned volumes complete); thousands more unpublished
**Document types:** Royal correspondence, treaties, legal transactions, astrological reports, oracle queries, prophetic oracles, administrative records, scholarly letters, grants, votive donations
**Translation status:** Fully translated for all published SAA volumes (transliteration + English translation with critical apparatus)
**Digital availability:** Freely available — State Archives of Assyria Online (SAAo) via Oracc (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/saao/)
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/saao/

**Behavioral economics signals (by volume):**
- **SAA 01 (Letters from Assyria and the West):** Administrative letters documenting commodity supplies, shortfalls, and grievances — loss-framing language
- **SAA 04 (Queries to the Sungod):** Extispicy oracle queries — explicit forecast requests about outcomes of decisions under uncertainty; unique forecast-request record
- **SAA 05 (Letters from the Northern and Northeastern Provinces):** Supply chain reports, price references
- **SAA 08 (Astrological Reports):** Systematic celestial observations with behavioral/political predictions — forecast corpus; celestial omens linked to outcome expectations
- **SAA 09 (Assyrian Prophecies):** Prophetic oracles delivering conditional predictions — "If the king does X, god Y will ensure Z"
- **SAA 10 (Letters from Assyrian and Babylonian Scholars):** Scholarly letters interpreting omens, advising on risk — explicit probabilistic reasoning about outcomes
- **SAA 15–19, 21:** Administrative, legal, and correspondence volumes with price data, commodity flows, named officials
- Named repeated agents: extensive prosopographical data (Prosopography of the Neo-Assyrian Empire, PNA)
- Conditional instructions: ubiquitous in oracle queries and letters
- Price data: commodity prices in administrative volumes

**Assessment:** A well-published, fully translated, and freely available online corpus of exceptional quality. The oracle query volumes (SAA04) and astrological reports (SAA08) are unique in all of antiquity as documented forecast-request records — agents asking deities to resolve uncertainty, then recording the query. This creates a partial forecast corpus. The scholarly letters (SAA10) show epistemic reasoning about uncertainty. **High behavioral relevance**, particularly for overconfidence and forecast/outcome pairing. Volumes already in hand for this project (SAA01, 05, 15–19, 21); SAA04, 08, 09, 10 should be added.

### 1.13 Neo-Assyrian Astrological and Oracle Volumes (SAA04, SAA08, SAA09, SAA10)

*These four volumes are treated separately given their unique forecast-record character.*

**Abbreviations:** SAA04 (State Archives of Assyria vol. 4); SAA08; SAA09; SAA10
**Date range:** c. 700–609 BCE (Esarhaddon and Assurbanipal periods primarily)
**Language and script:** Neo-Assyrian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Nineveh palace archives (Kuyunjik), Iraq
**Approximate size:** SAA04: ~350 texts (extispicy queries); SAA08: ~500 astrological reports; SAA09: ~32 prophetic texts; SAA10: ~278 scholarly letters
**Document types:**
- SAA04: Liver divination queries put to the sun-god before decisions (military, personal, diplomatic)
- SAA08: Regular astrological observation reports with omen-based predictions
- SAA09: Prophetic oracles delivered by named prophets
- SAA10: Correspondence of scholars advising on omen interpretation and risk
**Translation status:** Fully translated (SAA series standard)
**Digital availability:** Freely available via SAAo (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/saao/)
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/saao/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Forecast records**: SAA04 queries are formal requests for binary predictions (will campaign X succeed? will patient Y recover?). Each query is a structured forecast request.
- **Omen-outcome structure**: SAA08 records celestial events paired with predicted outcomes — enables analysis of whether predicted events materialized
- **Expert overconfidence potential**: Scholars in SAA10 express certainty levels about omen interpretation — disagreements between scholars are explicit
- **Conditional predictions**: "If planet X appears in position Y, then outcome Z will follow"
- **Named forecasters** (diviners, astrologers, prophets) who can be tracked across volumes
- **Decision framing**: Extispicy queries reveal what outcomes the decision-maker considered most salient (loss vs. gain framing)
- **Risk aversion in ritual context**: The act of divination itself reveals risk aversion — leaders sought divine confirmation before uncertain actions

**Assessment:** Unique in the ancient record. These four SAA volumes constitute the only known systematic forecast archive from the ancient world where named experts made structured predictions recorded before the outcome. SAA04 queries are especially valuable as they reveal what the decision-maker perceived as the key uncertain variable. **Highest possible behavioral signal for overconfidence and forecast bias research.** Priority acquisition for this project.

### 1.14 Neo-Babylonian Private Archives: Murashû and Egibi (c. 700–400 BCE)

**Abbreviations (Murashû):** Mur.; PBS (Publications of the Babylonian Section); IMT (Inschriften aus Nippur)
**Abbreviations (Egibi):** Egb.; BM (British Museum); Achemenet
**Date range:** Egibi: c. 606–484 BCE; Murashû: c. 455–403 BCE
**Language and script:** Late Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Egibi: Babylon (found by locals 1870s–1880s, dispersed to British Museum and other collections); Murashû: Nippur (excavated 1888–1900, primarily at University of Pennsylvania)
**Approximate size:** Egibi: ~1,700–2,000 tablets (estimates vary; up to 4,000 if fragments included); Murashû: ~879 tablets
**Document types:** Business contracts (loans, leases, sales, deposits), receipts, administrative records of large private businesses (Egibi: banking-like operations; Murashû: agricultural estate management)
**Translation status:** Partially translated — Murashû: key early publications by Clay (1904, 1912); modern studies by Stolper; Egibi: Wunsch's ongoing work; Achemenet hosts ~80 Egibi texts online
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Penn Museum has some Murashû online; Achemenet (http://www.achemenet.com) has Egibi texts; CDLI has records
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth; http://www.achemenet.com (Egibi); https://www.penn.museum/collections (Murashû)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Loan and debt records**: both archives are saturated with interest-bearing loans — excellent for risk preference and time preference analysis
- **Named recurring agents**: Egibi family traceable across five generations (c. 700–484 BCE); Murashû family traceable across two generations
- Commodity price data in contracts (barley, dates, sesame, silver)
- Agricultural risk management: Murashû managed estates under conditions of uncertain harvests
- Loss framing: debt restructuring and default records
- Cross-period data: Egibi spans Neo-Babylonian and Persian periods, enabling analysis of how behavioral patterns changed across political upheaval (Babylonian → Persian conquest)
- Named Jewish and Iranian individuals in Murashû records (demographic diversity)

**Assessment:** Two of the most important private business archives in all of antiquity. The Egibi archive is the longest-running private business record in the ancient world (five generations, >120 years). Both archives are rich in loan instruments and price data. **High behavioral relevance** for risk preference, time preference, and debt-distress behavior. The Egibi multi-generational span enables cohort analysis.

### 1.15 Babylonian Astronomical Diaries (c. 652 BCE – 61 CE)

**Abbreviations:** AD; ADART (Astronomical Diaries and Related Texts from Babylonia); ADsD
**Date range:** c. 652 BCE – 61 CE (with significant gaps; best coverage c. 385–61 BCE)
**Language and script:** Late Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Babylon (Esagila temple); tablets in British Museum (primary) and other collections
**Approximate size:** ~1,000 principal diary tablets; related texts (goal-year texts, eclipse records, planetary texts) bring total to ~2,000+ cuneiform records
**Document types:** Regular (approximately monthly) observations of: celestial events (planets, moon, eclipses), weather, river levels, commodity prices (barley, dates, mustard, cress, sesame, wool), and historical events
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — ADART volumes I–VI (Sachs/Hunger) published; ADsD project (Oracc) provides digital editions; ~700+ published tablets
**Digital availability:** Freely available via Oracc ADsD project (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/adsd/)
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/adsd/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Price series**: the longest continuous commodity price series in the pre-modern world — six commodities tracked across ~600 years (652 BCE–61 CE). Barley, dates, mustard, cress, sesame, and wool prices recorded monthly.
- **Forecast records**: astrological predictions paired with subsequent observations — enables omen accuracy assessment
- **Market outcome records**: price data linked to weather, flood, and historical events — enables shock-response analysis
- **Reference price extraction**: month-to-month price changes identifiable; seasonal patterns and deviations from reference prices calculable
- Celestial event records with predicted outcomes: enables overconfidence analysis if predictions are compared to realizations
- Named scholars at Esagila: some individual authorship traceable across tablets

**Assessment:** The most important single source for ancient commodity price series. The Babylonian price data have been used by economic historians (Slotsky, van der Spek, Pirngruber) and are the basis of a substantial scholarly literature on ancient market efficiency. The combination of price series and concurrent celestial event records (with associated predictions) makes this corpus uniquely suited to studying behavioral responses to market shocks. **Highest behavioral relevance** for price-series analysis and prospect theory applications. The ADsD Oracc project makes this freely accessible.

### 1.16 Persepolis Fortification Tablets and Treasury Tablets (c. 509–458 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** PFT; PTT; PFA (Persepolis Fortification Archive); PF (Fortification tablets per Hallock 1969)
**Date range:** PFT: c. 509–494 BCE; PTT: c. 492–458 BCE
**Language and script:** Elamite; cuneiform. Also ~500–1,000 in Aramaic script.
**Provenance:** Persepolis treasury fortification wall, Iran; excavated by Oriental Institute 1933–1934
**Approximate size:** PFT: ~15,000–18,000 cuneiform tablets (of which ~2,087 published by Hallock 1969; ~30,000 total pieces if fragments counted); PTT: ~800 tablets
**Document types:** Ration records for workers, travelers, and animals throughout the Achaemenid empire; commodity distributions (grain, wine, beer, meat, livestock); treasury receipts
**Translation status:** Minimally translated — Hallock published 2,087 Elamite tablets with translation (1969); the remaining ~13,000 cuneiform tablets are unpublished; draft editions of ~500 Aramaic texts exist
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Persepolis Fortification Archive Project (https://voices.uchicago.edu/ochre/project/pfa/); CDLI has records; Achemenet has high-resolution images
**Primary digital home:** https://isac.uchicago.edu/research/projects/persepolis-fortification-archive

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Ration allocations for diverse social groups (workers, craftsmen, travelers, animals) — institutional reference-price analysis
- Commodity quantity data at imperial scale
- Cross-regional price and ration comparisons possible (empire-wide distribution system)
- Named individual recipients in ration records
- Repeated administrative officials across tablet series
- Some conditional clauses in administrative orders

**Assessment:** The largest archive from the Achaemenid Persian empire. The ration data provide a unique cross-empire snapshot of commodity allocation at a fixed moment. However, the low publication rate (2,087 of ~18,000 tablets) is a major constraint. The published tablets are a non-random sample. Medium behavioral signal from published materials; high potential once more texts are edited. Most useful for institutional allocation and reference-price analysis.

### 1.17 Seleucid / Hellenistic Babylonian Archives (c. 330–63 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** SelBab; HBTIN; BALT; BRM (Babylonian Records in the Library of J. Pierpont Morgan)
**Date range:** c. 330–63 BCE (Alexander conquest through late Seleucid/early Parthian)
**Language and script:** Late Babylonian Akkadian; cuneiform
**Provenance:** Uruk, Babylon, Sippar, Nippur, and other Babylonian sites
**Approximate size:** ~1,500 legal, scholarly, and religious cuneiform documents from this period; BALT project covers 2,990 administrative and legal texts across Neo-Babylonian, Persian, and Hellenistic periods (c. 626–93 BCE)
**Document types:** Legal contracts (sales, rentals, loans), administrative records, astronomical diaries (see 1.15), temple records, bilingual scholarly texts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — key archives in BRM series and Yale Babylonian Collection publications; HBTIN (Oracc) project provides editions
**Digital availability:** Partially available — HBTIN (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/hbtin/), SelBI (Seleucid Building Inscriptions), BALT (Zenodo)
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/hbtin/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Legal contracts with commodity price data in silver
- Loan documents (interest rates, collateral, default clauses)
- Named individuals in repeated transactions
- Cross-cultural documentation: Babylonian legal practices under Greek and Macedonian political control — potential for analysis of how reference prices adapted to new rulers
- Temple archives show institutional continuity despite political change (stability bias?)
- Astronomical Diaries overlap this period (see 1.15)

**Assessment:** A valuable bridge corpus connecting Neo-Babylonian and Roman-era records. The Hellenistic period in Babylonia is particularly interesting because local Akkadian legal and commercial practices persisted alongside Greek political institutions — a natural experiment in institutional persistence. Medium-high behavioral signal, particularly in loan documents and cross-cultural price adaptation.

### 1.18 Hittite Archives — Hattusa/Boğazköy (c. 1650–1180 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** KBo (Keilschrifttexte aus Boghazköi); KUB (Keilschrifturkunden aus Boghazköi); CTH (Catalogue des Textes Hittites); ChD (Chicago Hittite Dictionary)
**Date range:** c. 1650–1180 BCE (Old through New Hittite Kingdom; archive destroyed c. 1180 BCE)
**Language and script:** Hittite (an Indo-European language); also Akkadian, Hurrian, Luwian, Palaic, and Hattic; all in cuneiform
**Provenance:** Hattusa (modern Boğazköy / Boğazkale), central Anatolia (Turkey); excavated by German missions since 1906; excavations ongoing
**Approximate size:** ~30,000 tablets and fragments from the Boğazköy archives; UNESCO registered; ~10,000 text compositions catalogued in CTH (since 1971); substantial portions remain unpublished
**Document types:** Royal annals, international treaties (some of the earliest known), diplomatic correspondence, legal codes, palace administrative inventories, cult ritual instructions, omen texts, military logistics records, land grants
**Translation status:** Partially translated — major legal and treaty texts are well studied; administrative and inventory texts partially published in KBo/KUB series and through the ISAC (Chicago) Hittite Dictionary project; the Thesaurus Linguarum Hethaeorum Digitalis (TLHdig, launched 2023 on Hethitologie-Portal Mainz) now provides online lemmatized access to published texts
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Hethitologie-Portal Mainz (https://www.hethport.uni-wuerzburg.de/HPM/) hosts the TLHdig platform and corpus resources; CDLI has records; the electronic Chicago Hittite Dictionary (e-CHD) is searchable at the ISAC website (https://isac.uchicago.edu/research/projects/hit/chicago-hittite-dictionary-project)
**Primary digital home:** https://www.hethport.uni-wuerzburg.de/HPM/ (Hethitologie-Portal Mainz); https://isac.uchicago.edu/research/projects/hit (e-CHD)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- International treaties with conditional clauses specifying obligations and consequences ("if you do X, then Y will follow") — some of the most explicit ancient conditional-commitment documents
- Diplomatic correspondence records gift-exchange equivalencies and complaint letters about insufficient reciprocity (structurally similar to Amarna letters)
- Palace inventory and administrative texts: commodity allocations and ration equivalencies
- Land grant documents with explicit reference points (prior holdings)
- Military logistics texts: supply requisitions with expected vs. actual deliveries
- Omen texts: structured forecast queries (similar in function to Neo-Assyrian oracle queries)
- Named officials in administrative rosters

**Assessment:** The Hittite archive is the largest Bronze Age administrative corpus from Anatolia. The treaty corpus is uniquely valuable for studying conditional commitment under uncertainty at the inter-state level. Administrative and inventory texts provide commodity allocation data. The omen texts parallel the Neo-Assyrian oracle query genre. The relatively low publication rate and the primarily Hittite-language barrier (less widely studied than Akkadian) somewhat limit access, but TLHdig (2023) substantially improves digital availability. **Medium behavioral signal** overall; the treaty and omen texts score higher; the administrative inventories are useful for reference-price benchmarking.

### 1.19 Proto-Elamite Tablets (c. 3100–2900 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** PE; Proto-Elamite
**Date range:** c. 3100–2900 BCE
**Language and script:** Proto-Elamite script; undeciphered logographic system; inscribed on clay tablets
**Provenance:** Susa (Iran; >1,500 tablets), Malyan (~30), Tepe Yahya (27), Sialk (22), and scattered other Iranian sites; also Shahr-i Sokhta and Jiroft; many held at the Louvre
**Approximate size:** ~1,600 tablets known (some estimates slightly higher); substantially smaller than Proto-cuneiform, though possibly representing the same administrative impulse in a parallel tradition
**Document types:** Administrative accounts (commodity counts, ration records, livestock tallies) — all content inferred from numerical and sign structure; no prose recoverable
**Translation status:** Not translated — the script remains undeciphered; numerical portions are understood; sign values are largely unknown; structural analysis has identified administrative functions (accounting, redistribution) by analogy with Proto-cuneiform
**Digital availability:** Freely available — CDLI (https://cdli.earth) hosts the full published corpus with high-resolution images; Oxford/Southampton RTI imaging project (2012) produced RTI images of 1,000+ tablets in the Louvre collection, accessible via CDLI
**Primary digital home:** https://cdli.earth (search: Proto-Elamite)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Numerical records of commodity quantities (inferred): enable ratio and allocation analysis
- Administrative redistribution patterns identifiable from structural analysis
- No named individuals recoverable; no letter or complaint genre possible given undeciphered status
- Parallel temporal coverage to the Late Uruk administrative tablets — comparative analysis possible

**Assessment:** Proto-Elamite is the earliest large-scale administrative writing system outside Mesopotamia, roughly contemporary with late Proto-cuneiform. The undeciphered state precludes any recovery of language, named agents, or explicit economic reasoning. The corpus is documented here for completeness; it establishes that administrative accounting practices emerged independently (or diffused very early) across a wide geographic range. **No direct behavioral signal** in the individual-bias sense; valuable as a structural baseline for early administrative quantification.

### 1.20 Urartian Inscriptions (c. 860–590 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** CTU (Corpus dei testi urartei, Salvini); eCUT (Electronic Corpus of Urartian Texts)
**Date range:** c. 860–590 BCE (Kingdom of Urartu; destroyed c. 590 BCE by Scythians and Medes)
**Language and script:** Urartian (Hurro-Urartian language family, unrelated to Semitic or Indo-European); cuneiform borrowed from Assyrian
**Provenance:** Lake Van region, eastern Anatolia (modern Turkey), northwestern Iran, and Armenia; inscriptions on stone, bronze, and clay; tablets from Karmir Blur (Teishebaini fortress) particularly important
**Approximate size:** ~500 inscriptions and texts catalogued in CTU I–V (Salvini, 2008–2018); the administrative clay tablet archive from Karmir Blur numbers several dozen tablets
**Document types:** Royal annals and building inscriptions (stone and rock faces); administrative clay tablets (ration distributions, livestock tallies); dedications on bronze objects; a handful of letters
**Translation status:** Substantially translated for the major inscriptions — CTU I–V provides the standard edition; eCUT (Electronic Corpus of Urartian Texts, LMU Munich) provides annotated transliterations, English translations, and a glossary for the full published corpus
**Digital availability:** Freely available via eCUT (https://www.en.ag.geschichte.uni-muenchen.de/research/ecut/index.html), which is linked from CDLI as an external resource; the CTU corpora are the basis for eCUT editions
**Primary digital home:** https://www.en.ag.geschichte.uni-muenchen.de/research/ecut/index.html (eCUT)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative tablets from Karmir Blur: ration and livestock distribution records with quantities — institutional reference-point data
- Royal annals: booty and tribute quantification (commodities seized or received) — implicit price-equivalency references
- Named officials in administrative records
- Annals record campaign outcomes in explicit terms (gain framing: "I took X sheep, Y cattle, Z captives")
- No private correspondence; no merchant letters; no loan documents

**Assessment:** Urartu is an important Iron Age kingdom whose administrative records complement the better-known Assyrian and Babylonian corpora. The eCUT project makes the full published corpus freely available in translation. The administrative tablets provide ration-level commodity data; the annals offer structured outcome reports. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** — primarily useful for institutional allocation benchmarks and comparative Iron Age data alongside Neo-Assyrian sources.

## 2. Egyptian Corpora

### 2.1 Abusir Papyri (c. 2400 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Abusir Pap.
**Date range:** c. 2450–2350 BCE (Fifth Dynasty)
**Language and script:** Ancient Egyptian hieratic
**Provenance:** Abusir (ancient Abu Sir), near Cairo; Czech excavations at Neferefre pyramid complex found ~2,000 fragments in 1970s; earlier fragments in Berlin, London
**Approximate size:** Several hundred pieces from earlier finds; ~2,000 separate fragments from the Neferefre complex
**Document types:** Mortuary temple administrative records — duty rosters, offering lists, equipment inventories, letters and permits
**Translation status:** Partially translated — key publications by Posener-Kriéger (1976); Czech Institute of Egyptology ongoing work; select volumes with English commentary
**Digital availability:** Minimally available digitally — Czech Institute of Egyptology publishes select volumes as open-access PDFs
**Primary digital home:** https://cegu.ff.cuni.cz (Czech Egyptological Institute)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Duty rosters: scheduled vs. actual attendance records (absence = potential loss-framing evidence)
- Offering and ration allocations by rank
- Equipment inventory changes over time
- Priest rotation schedules with conditional clauses

**Assessment:** The oldest surviving administrative papyri. The duty rosters are interesting as attendance records that could reveal systematic patterns in absence behavior. Translation access is limited. Low-to-medium behavioral signal due to limited commercial content; primary value is in establishing institutional allocation patterns at the dawn of Egyptian documentary history.

### 2.2 Heqanakht Letters (c. 2002 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Heqanakht Pap.; P. Heqanakht
**Date range:** c. 2002 BCE (early Middle Kingdom, Mentuhotep IV or early Amenemhat I)
**Language and script:** Ancient Egyptian hieratic
**Provenance:** Theban necropolis (found in tomb of Meseh, 1921–22, by Metropolitan Museum expedition)
**Approximate size:** 5 letters and 4 accounts (9 documents total)
**Document types:** Private letters from a ka-priest to his household managing agricultural land and rations; 4 accounts of income and expenditure
**Translation status:** Fully translated — James (1962), Goedicke (1984), Allen (2002); multiple competing translations
**Digital availability:** Freely available — Metropolitan Museum digital collections (https://metmuseum.org); PDF of Allen 2002 edition
**Primary digital home:** https://libmma.contentdm.oclc.org/digital/collection/p15324coll10/id/177421/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Loss aversion language**: Heqanakht's letters explicitly express anxiety about losing land and assets; tone is one of excessive worry about small losses
- **Reference-point setting**: explicit discussions of fair ration levels and complaints when allocations fall short
- **Complaint letters** par excellence: Heqanakht scolds household members for overspending; frames shortfalls as losses
- Conditional instructions: explicit ("If Sihathor arrives before the inundation, give him this and that")
- Agricultural risk management: flood-year vs. drought-year strategies discussed
- Named individuals with recurring roles
- Scholars (notably James Allen) have noted the letters' psychological vividness — the writer's voice is exceptionally personal

**Assessment:** A tiny but behaviorally rich corpus. The Heqanakht letters are among the most psychologically revealing documents in all of Egyptian literature — a real person's anxious, loss-averse voice preserved across four millennia. Though only 9 documents, they provide a case study in loss framing and reference-point sensitivity that can anchor qualitative behavioral analysis. **Medium-high behavioral relevance** given size; unique for qualitative loss-aversion illustration.

### 2.3 Lahun / Kahun Papyri (c. 1850 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Kah. Pap.; P. Lahun; Griffith Papyri
**Date range:** c. 1897–1786 BCE (Middle Kingdom, primarily Amenemhat III)
**Language and script:** Ancient Egyptian hieratic
**Provenance:** Kahun (ancient Lahun), Fayum, Egypt; excavated by Flinders Petrie 1889; held at University College London (Petrie Museum)
**Approximate size:** ~1,000 fragments covering medical, mathematical, administrative, religious, and legal topics
**Document types:** Administrative records, town accounts, legal texts, mathematical problems, medical papyri, letters, veterinary texts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — Griffith 1898 published key texts; UCL digital Egypt project has selected translations; medical and mathematical texts well translated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — UCL Digital Egypt (https://www.ucl.ac.uk/museums-static/digitalegypt/); some texts on Petrie Museum site
**Primary digital home:** https://www.ucl.ac.uk/museums-static/digitalegypt/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative town accounts: commodity allocations and labor records
- Legal texts: land and property transactions with price data
- Mathematical papyri: ration and division problems (establish period numerical reasoning practices)
- Letters: private correspondence with supply grievances
- Named individuals in administrative accounts

**Assessment:** A diverse Middle Kingdom archive with administrative and legal content. The town accounts provide insight into communal resource management. The legal texts have price and property data. Medium behavioral signal; value in establishing Middle Kingdom reference prices and administrative allocation patterns.

### 2.4 Deir el-Medina Ostraca and Papyri (c. 1550–1070 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** DM; Dém.; DMD (Deir el-Medina Database); O.DM (ostraca)
**Date range:** c. 1550–1070 BCE (New Kingdom, Dynasty XVIII–XX)
**Language and script:** Ancient Egyptian hieratic and hieroglyphic; Demotic in later material
**Provenance:** Deir el-Medina (ancient Set-Ma'at), west bank Luxor (Thebes), Egypt — the village of royal tomb workers
**Approximate size:** ~5,000+ ostraca; hundreds of papyri; DMD database contains data from >5,000 documents (>4,000 online); thousands more await publication
**Document types:** Administrative ostraca (attendance records, ration distributions, work assignments), private letters, legal documents (land sales, debt, inheritance), literary ostraca, census records, payment and delivery records
**Translation status:** Partially translated — major collections published in KRI (Kitchen, Ramesside Inscriptions) and KRIP (translations); DMD provides references to editions; many ostraca untranslated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — DMD (https://dmd.wepwawet.nl/) is a searchable non-literary text database; TPOP (Turin Papyrus Online Platform) covers Turin-held material
**Primary digital home:** https://dmd.wepwawet.nl/; https://collezionepapiri.museoegizio.it

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Attendance and labor records**: month-by-month records of who worked and who was absent, with reasons — enables analysis of work/shirk decisions
- **Ration distribution records**: regular rations with documented shortfalls — complaint behavior documented
- **Turin Strike Papyrus** (see 2.5): the world's first recorded organized labor action, explicitly framed around withheld rations
- **Private letters**: interpersonal financial disputes, debt grievances, loss-framing language
- **Legal documents**: land and property sales with price data; debt contracts
- Named individuals: thousands of named workers and officials, many traceable across decades
- **Repeated-agent analysis**: individual workers' attendance records span careers — the longest-running individual-level labor dataset in antiquity
- Census records: household composition changes over time

**Assessment:** One of the most behaviorally rich Egyptian corpora. Deir el-Medina is unique in ancient history as a community whose documentary record spans >400 years of ordinary working people's lives. The combination of attendance records, ration complaints, debt documents, and strike records provides direct evidence of loss aversion, reference-point sensitivity (rations as reference), and collective action in response to perceived unfair treatment. **High behavioral relevance**, particularly for loss aversion, reference wages, and labor-market behavior.

### 2.5 Turin Strike Papyrus (c. 1157 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** P. Turin Cat. 1880; Turin Strike Pap.
**Date range:** Year 29 of Ramesses III (c. 1157 BCE)
**Language and script:** Ancient Egyptian hieratic
**Provenance:** Deir el-Medina (same village as 2.4); now at Museo Egizio, Turin
**Approximate size:** 1 papyrus (single document, though a major text)
**Document types:** Administrative report describing work stoppages and sit-ins
**Translation status:** Fully translated — multiple English, French, and German translations; Edgerton (1951) classic edition
**Digital availability:** Freely available — Museo Egizio online collection (https://collezionepapiri.museoegizio.it/en-GB/document/131/) and TPOP platform
**Primary digital home:** https://collezionepapiri.museoegizio.it/en-GB/document/131/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Direct loss-aversion evidence**: workers strike because rations fall below established reference level
- **Reference point documentation**: the papyrus explicitly notes that rations had not been paid and workers could not continue without them
- **Collective action response to perceived unfairness**: workers cite entitlement to rations as the basis for action
- **Outcome-framing**: the scribe frames the situation as workers suffering a deprivation (loss frame)
- Conditional government responses: officials promise rations contingent on return to work

**Assessment:** A single document, but it is the most direct ancient evidence of reference-point-based behavioral response available anywhere in the record. Workers acted collectively because their experienced ration level served as a reference point below which continued work was unacceptable — a textbook loss-aversion scenario. **Very high behavioral signal** for qualitative analysis of loss aversion and reference wages, despite tiny corpus size.

### 2.6 Elephantine Papyri (Aramaic, c. 495–399 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** AP (Aramaic Papyri); TAD (Textbook of Aramaic Documents from Ancient Egypt); Eleph.
**Date range:** c. 495–399 BCE (Persian period in Egypt)
**Language and script:** Imperial Aramaic; papyrus and leather
**Provenance:** Elephantine (Yeb) and Syene (Aswan), Upper Egypt; found by locals 1890s and excavated by German mission; materials in Berlin, Paris, New York, and Cairo
**Approximate size:** TAD contains ~700+ texts; ERC Elephantine project has catalogued >4,000 new objects (80% previously unpublished) from Berlin, Paris, and New York
**Document types:** Legal contracts (marriage, divorce, property, loans), administrative texts, letters, literary texts; documents of a Jewish military garrison community
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — TAD volumes 1–4 provide full edition with translation; Porten and Yardeni standard reference; new ERC project materials being published
**Digital availability:** Partially available — ERC Elephantine project database (https://elephantine.smb.museum/) provides open-access online database with metadata and transcriptions
**Primary digital home:** https://elephantine.smb.museum/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Legal contracts**: marriage contracts, property transfers, loan documents with explicit terms
- **Named individuals** in a defined community (Jewish garrison + Egyptian neighbors + Persian officials) traceable across documents
- **Loan and debt contracts**: interest terms and collateral documented
- **Property dispute records**: loss-framing in claims
- **Cross-cultural transactions**: Jew-Egyptian-Persian interactions — potential for analysis of in-group/out-group risk preferences
- **Letters**: interpersonal disputes, supply complaints

**Assessment:** A community archive covering a defined population over ~100 years. The legal and loan documents are well-suited for risk preference analysis. The community's Jewish identity and Persian administrative context creates interesting cross-cultural dimensions. Medium-high behavioral signal for community-level behavioral analysis.

### 2.7 Zenon Archive (Ptolemaic, c. 259–229 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Zen.; Zenon Pap.; PSI (Papiri della Società Italiana)
**Date range:** c. 261–229 BCE (Ptolemy II Philadelphus)
**Language and script:** Greek; papyrus
**Provenance:** Philadelphia (Fayyum), Egypt; found by local diggers c. 1915; dispersed to collections in Egypt, France, Italy, UK, USA, and elsewhere
**Approximate size:** ~1,800+ texts (the largest Ptolemaic private archive)
**Document types:** Personal and official letters, accounts, contracts, petitions, lists — the complete business records of Zenon, estate manager for Apollonius (finance minister of Ptolemy II)
**Translation status:** Partially translated — Edgar's early editions (1920s); Michigan Zenon Papyri project (English); many texts translated in specialized studies; not all 1,800 are translated into English
**Digital availability:** Partially available — papyri.info/Duke Databank includes many Zenon texts; British Library has digitized its Zenon holdings
**Primary digital home:** https://papyri.info (search: Zenon); https://apps.lib.umich.edu/reading/Zenon/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **The most complete private business archive of the ancient Mediterranean**: single agent (Zenon) managing estate investments across 30+ years
- Letters document price expectations, market intelligence, investment decisions
- Accounts enable expectation vs. outcome comparison
- Petitions: loss-framing, complaints about fair dealing
- Named repeated agents: extensive — Zenon corresponds with dozens of named traders and officials
- Commodity price data (wheat, oil, livestock, wine, textiles)
- Cross-regional trade: Zenon traveled from Syria to Egypt — comparative price data across markets

**Assessment:** The Zenon archive is the closest ancient equivalent to a modern manager's email archive — one person's complete professional correspondence. The 30-year span and business focus make it ideal for studying how a single agent's expectations and risk preferences evolved. **High behavioral relevance** for individual-level bias analysis; under-exploited by economists.

### 2.8 Roman Egypt Papyri / Oxyrhynchus and APIS/DDbDP (30 BCE – 640 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Oxy.; APIS; DDbDP; BGU (Berliner griechische Urkunden); P.Mich.
**Date range:** 30 BCE – 640 CE (Roman and early Byzantine Egypt)
**Language and script:** Greek (primary), Latin, Coptic, Demotic, Arabic (late)
**Provenance:** Oxyrhynchus (Bahnasa), Alexandria, Fayum, and sites throughout Egypt
**Approximate size:** DDbDP: ~50,000 texts; Oxyrhynchus: ~6,000 published (from an estimated 500,000+ fragments); APIS covers >100,000 images across multiple collections
**Document types:** Private letters, tax records, census documents, contracts (loan, lease, sale), petitions, court records, price lists, official correspondence, legal documents
**Translation status:** Oxyrhynchus: fully translated for all 6,000 published texts; DDbDP texts variably translated; overall the best-published Greek papyrus corpus
**Digital availability:** Freely available — papyri.info integrates DDbDP, HGV, APIS, and other databases; P.Oxy. images from vol. 47 onward on Oxford portal
**Primary digital home:** https://papyri.info; https://oxyrhynchus.web.ox.ac.uk

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Largest ancient documentary database**: ~50,000 texts covering 670 years of economic life
- **Tax records**: annual papyri with household assessments — reference-point benchmarking possible
- **Price lists**: market prices documented across centuries — the longest continuous Greek-language price series
- **Loan and debt documents**: extensive corpus of credit instruments with interest rates
- **Petitions**: complaint documents with explicit loss-framing; appeals to fairness norms
- **Private letters**: interpersonal financial disputes, investment decisions, supply chains
- **Named repeated agents**: many families traceable across decades in local archives
- **Stratonomics**: census-linked data enabling household-level economic analysis
- Price data linkable to historical events (political disruption, harvest failure) for shock analysis

**Assessment:** By volume, this is the most important documentary source for the ancient Mediterranean economy. The 670-year span makes price-series analysis feasible; the 50,000+ documents include thousands of loan and debt instruments. Papyri.info makes this the most digitally accessible major ancient corpus. **Highest behavioral relevance** for quantitative analysis; the scale enables statistical approaches impossible with smaller corpora.

## 3. Levantine and West Semitic Corpora

### 3.1 Amarna Letters (c. 1360–1332 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** EA (El-Amarna); AM; Amarna Dip.
**Date range:** c. 1360–1332 BCE (Amenhotep III through Tutankhamun)
**Language and script:** Akkadian (with Canaanite glosses); cuneiform on clay tablets
**Provenance:** Tell el-Amarna (ancient Akhetaten), Middle Egypt; found by locals 1887; dispersed to Berlin, London, Cairo, and other museums
**Approximate size:** ~382 tablets and fragments (350 letters + 4 administrative texts + tablets)
**Document types:** International diplomatic correspondence between Egypt and Mesopotamian kingdoms (Babylon, Assyria, Mitanni, Hatti), and between Egypt and Canaanite vassal states
**Translation status:** Fully translated — Moran (1992) standard English translation; Rainey (2015) critical edition
**Digital availability:** Freely available — Oracc Amarna Letters project (https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu); CDLI records; archive.org has Moran text
**Primary digital home:** https://oracc.museum.upenn.edu (search: Amarna)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Diplomatic gift-exchange records**: explicit royal "gift" transactions record equivalencies between commodities (gold, lapis lazuli, horses, chariots) — embedded price benchmarks
- **Complaint letters**: vassal rulers complain about insufficient gifts (loss framing at royal level)
- **Reference-price anchoring**: letters frequently reference past gift amounts as benchmarks for current requests
- **Conditional instructions**: kings set conditions for alliances contingent on gift reciprocity
- Named royal agents: both senders and recipients are historically documented
- Status quo bias potential: vassal kings resist changing tribute terms

**Assessment:** A small but uniquely documented corpus of international diplomatic economic behavior. The gift-exchange system is well understood theoretically, and the complaint letters provide explicit evidence of reference-price sensitivity and loss framing at the diplomatic level. Fully translated and freely available online. **Medium-high behavioral signal** for reference dependence and loss aversion in diplomatic contexts.

### 3.3 Samaria Ostraca (c. 786–746 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Sam. Ost.; KAI 183–188 (in Donner & Röllig corpus)
**Date range:** c. 786–746 BCE (reign of Jeroboam II, Israel)
**Language and script:** Paleo-Hebrew (cursive)
**Provenance:** Royal acropolis of Samaria (Sebastos), northern Israel; excavated by Harvard University 1908–1910
**Approximate size:** 102 ostraca recovered; ~63 legible; ~60 published by Reisner
**Document types:** Administrative records of shipments of wine and olive oil to the royal palace
**Translation status:** Fully translated — Reisner (1924), Yadin (1961), modern editions; all legible texts translated
**Digital availability:** Physically held at Istanbul Archaeological Museum; digital images based on 1910 Harvard glass negatives; multispectral imaging planned
**Primary digital home:** No dedicated open-access digital portal; images in academic publications

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Commodity shipment records with quantities (wine jars, oil quantities)
- Named estate managers and recipients
- Year-by-year records enable seasonal/annual comparison
- Limited price data (quantities not prices recorded)

**Assessment:** A small administrative corpus documenting tax-in-kind payments to a royal center. The named estate managers and recurring recipients provide a modest network for repeated-agent analysis. No price data, which limits behavioral analysis. **Low behavioral signal** for bias detection; value is in establishing reference-quantity benchmarks for Israelite palace economy.

### 3.4 Lachish Letters (c. 587 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Lachish Ost.; KAI 192–199
**Date range:** c. 588–587 BCE (eve of Babylonian conquest of Judah)
**Language and script:** Paleo-Hebrew (archaic)
**Provenance:** Gate complex of Lachish (Tell ed-Duweir), southern Israel; excavated by J.L. Starkey 1935–1938
**Approximate size:** 21 ostraca; 18 legible; 17 at British Museum, others at Rockefeller Museum
**Document types:** Military correspondence — letters from field commander to garrison commander at Lachish during the Babylonian siege
**Translation status:** Fully translated — Torczyner (1938), Pardee (1982), various subsequent editions
**Digital availability:** Minimally — British Museum online collection has images for BM pieces
**Primary digital home:** https://www.britishmuseum.org/collection (search: Lachish)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Military reporting under extreme uncertainty (siege conditions)
- Loss-framing language: explicit references to towns falling to the enemy
- Conditional instructions from the commander
- Named military officers as agents
- Crisis decision-making context (behavioral distortions under stress)

**Assessment:** A tiny corpus of exceptional historical value but limited behavioral-economic utility. The crisis context (imminent military defeat) creates extreme conditions where normal bias patterns may not apply. The texts are fully translated and accessible. **Low behavioral signal** for standard behavioral analysis; potentially interesting for crisis-decision-making studies.

### 3.5 Babatha Archive and Cave of Letters Documents (c. 93–135 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Yadin; 5/6Hev; Judean Desert Studies (JDS)
**Date range:** 93–135 CE (Nabataean through Bar Kokhba period)
**Language and script:** Nabataean Aramaic, Jewish Aramaic, Greek; papyrus and leather
**Provenance:** Cave of Letters (Nahal Hever), Judean Desert, Israel; discovered by Yigael Yadin 1960
**Approximate size:** Babatha archive: 35 documents (P.Yadin 1–35); total Cave of Letters documents: ~150+
**Document types:** Legal contracts (marriage, guardianship, property registration, land lease, deposit), court petitions, census declarations
**Translation status:** Fully translated — JDS volumes 2 (Greek, 1989) and 3 (Aramaic, 2002); individual texts well studied
**Digital availability:** Minimally available digitally; JDS volumes partially on academic repositories
**Primary digital home:** https://www.nli.org.il (National Library of Israel); partial availability via academic databases

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Named individual**: Babatha's entire legal life documented — a single woman's property, marriage, and court disputes across 40 years
- **Property registration**: Roman census declaration with asset valuation — reference-point document
- **Court petitions**: explicit loss-framing ("I have suffered loss of X from Y")
- **Guardianship disputes**: framing of child's assets as protected reference
- Legal contracts with explicit monetary terms
- Cross-cultural legal context: Nabataean, Roman, and Jewish law applied to same transactions

**Assessment:** A small but behaviorally rich personal archive. Babatha's 35 documents trace one woman's legal and economic life from before marriage through the Bar Kokhba revolt. The guardianship petitions are textbook examples of loss-aversion framing in legal advocacy. The Roman census declaration provides a unique asset valuation baseline. **Medium-high behavioral signal** for qualitative case study; the court petition language is particularly valuable.

- Named recurring agents (subordinate commanders)

### 3.7 Arad Ostraca (c. 600 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** Arad Ost.; EA (Eliashib Archive)
**Date range:** c. 605–595 BCE (late Iron Age IIc; Judean monarchy, shortly before Babylonian conquest)
**Language and script:** Paleo-Hebrew (cursive); some Aramaic
**Provenance:** Tel Arad, Negev highlands, southern Israel; excavated by Yohanan Aharoni 1962–1967
**Approximate size:** ~200 ostraca total; 107 in Paleo-Hebrew assigned to the Iron Age; ~18 major administrative letters in the Eliashib Archive are well legible
**Document types:** Military supply correspondence addressed to Eliashib (fort quartermaster) — orders for wine, oil, flour, and bread; administrative letters from superior officers; troop movement reports; a small number of census-type records
**Translation status:** Fully translated — Aharoni (1981) standard edition; multiple subsequent English editions; texts are brief and well understood
**Digital availability:** Minimally available as open-access digital editions — Israel Museum holds the main collection; the Smithsonian / Biblical Archaeology Society has published transcriptions; digital images produced through multispectral imaging by Tel Aviv University researchers (results published in PNAS 2016 on literacy); no single open-access text portal
**Primary digital home:** No dedicated open-access portal; Israel Museum (https://www.imj.org.il) and academic publications

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Supply requisition records**: letters explicitly order specific quantities of commodities — reference-quantity benchmarking
- Named recurring agents: Eliashib appears across at least 18 letters as the sole recipient — a single quartermaster's complete administrative correspondence
- Conditional instructions: "give X rations to the Kittim" — standard conditional-distribution format
- Some letters show hierarchical authorization chains (loss of supply framing if not delivered)
- Military logistics under threat conditions: the archive ends just before the Babylonian conquest — potential crisis-period behavioral data

**Assessment:** A small but tightly defined archive. The Eliashib Archive is particularly valuable because all letters address one named individual — enabling a focused repeated-agent analysis of a military quartermaster's operations. The commodity records (wine, oil, flour allocations) provide reference-quantity data for a Judean military fort. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** due to tiny size and administrative-only content; valuable for complementing the Lachish and Samaria ostraca corpora.

### 3.8 Phoenician and Punic Inscriptions (c. 1050 BCE – 4th century CE)

**Abbreviations:** KAI (Kanaanäische und Aramäische Inschriften; Donner & Röllig); CIS (Corpus Inscriptionum Semiticarum); CIP (Corpus Inscriptionum Phoenicarum necnon Poenicarum)
**Date range:** c. 1050 BCE – 4th century CE (broadly; Punic in North Africa continues later)
**Language and script:** Phoenician and Punic (West Semitic alphabetic scripts, ancestral to Hebrew and Aramaic alphabets)
**Provenance:** Lebanon (Byblos, Sidon, Tyre), Cyprus, Sardinia, Sicily, Malta, North Africa (Carthage, Tripolitania), Spain, and throughout the western Mediterranean
**Approximate size:** >10,000 inscriptions known; CIP project has collected ~7,800 with ongoing additions; KAI published ~202 key texts; the ASOR Punic Project focuses on ~6,000 Carthaginian stelae
**Document types:** Votive and funerary inscriptions (majority); temple dedications; commercial contracts; tariff texts; royal building inscriptions; shipowner dedications; a small number of economic administrative texts; the Marseille Tariff (CIS I 165; KAI 69) and Carthage Tariff (KAI 74) document temple service fees
**Translation status:** Partially translated — KAI provides standard German transliterations and commentary for 202 key texts; English translations in Krahmalkov's Phoenician-Punic Grammar and Josephson/Lehmann; CIP project is digitizing and providing new editions; majority of 10,000 inscriptions are brief votive texts with little economic content
**Digital availability:** Partially available — CIP (Corpus Inscriptionum Phoenicarum, http://cip.cchs.csic.es/) is the main digital project; ASOR Punic Project (http://www.phoinikeia.org/) provides digital files and 3D models of Carthaginian stelae; PHI Greek Inscriptions includes some bilingual Phoenician-Greek texts
**Primary digital home:** http://cip.cchs.csic.es/ (CIP); http://www.phoinikeia.org/ (ASOR Punic)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Marseille Tariff** and **Carthage Tariff**: explicit price lists for sacrificial offerings and temple fees — among the oldest surviving formal price lists in the western Mediterranean (5th–4th century BCE)
- Commercial port inscriptions: shipowner dedications with implicit reference to maritime trade values
- Votive dedication quantification: weights of precious metals offered — asset valuation records
- Cross-Mediterranean commercial network documentation: Phoenician traders appear at sites from Lebanon to Spain, creating a potential for comparative price analysis
- Funerary inscriptions with asset quantification (rare but present)

**Assessment:** The Phoenician-Punic civilization was the dominant commercial maritime culture of the first millennium BCE Mediterranean, yet its documentary legacy is heavily skewed toward stone inscriptions rather than papyrus or tablet archives. The tariff texts are the most behaviorally relevant material — explicit price schedules that establish reference prices for religious services. The absence of merchant correspondence (no Phoenician equivalent to Old Assyrian letters has survived) is a significant limitation. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** from the epigraphic corpus as a whole; the tariff texts are specifically medium-high for price-reference analysis.

### 3.9 Saqqara Aramaic Papyri (c. 475–400 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** TAD B8 (Textbook of Aramaic Documents); Saqqara Aram.
**Date range:** c. 475–400 BCE (Persian period in Egypt)
**Language and script:** Imperial Aramaic; papyrus
**Provenance:** North Saqqara (Memphis region), Egypt; excavated by British Museum mission and found by dealers; texts published by J.B. Segal (1983) and partially in TAD
**Approximate size:** ~202 papyrus fragments and 26 ostraca in the Saqqara Aramaic corpus per Segal's catalogue; most are highly fragmentary; ~12 court protocol fragments (TAD B8.1–12) and a handful of letters are legible
**Document types:** Fragmentary court protocols, letters, and administrative documents from a Persian-period legal archive associated with the satrapal administration at Memphis; linguistic evidence suggests a mixed Egyptian-Persian-Semitic administrative environment
**Translation status:** Partially translated — major legible texts published by Segal (1983) with English commentary; TAD includes the court protocols (B8 series) and select letters; substantial portions are too fragmentary to translate
**Digital availability:** Minimally available — TAD volumes are the standard reference but not freely online; no dedicated digital portal; CDLI and ERC Elephantine project cover overlapping material but not specifically Saqqara
**Primary digital home:** Academic editions (Segal 1983; Porten/Yardeni TAD); no open-access digital portal

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Court protocols: legal dispute framing with explicit claim and counterclaim language
- Administrative correspondence: supply and requisition letters
- Cross-cultural context: Persian, Egyptian, and Semitic agents interacting in a satrapal legal setting
- Named individuals in court records

**Assessment:** A fragmentary and poorly preserved corpus that complements the Elephantine archive (section 2.6) as evidence for Imperial Aramaic administrative practice in Persian Egypt. The court protocols have some dispute-framing relevance, but the fragmentary state severely limits behavioral analysis. **Low behavioral signal** in current published state; primarily useful as a complement to the better-preserved Elephantine documents.

### 3.10 Wadi Daliyeh Papyri (c. 375–335 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** WDSP (Wadi Daliyeh Samaria Papyri); DJD XXVIII (Discoveries in the Judaean Desert, vol. 28)
**Date range:** c. 375–335 BCE (latest document: 19 March 335 BCE — just before Alexander's conquest)
**Language and script:** Imperial Aramaic; papyrus and clay bullae
**Provenance:** Wadi Daliyeh (cave of Abu Shinjeh), 14 km north of Jericho; discovered by Bedouin c. 1962; excavated by Paul Lapp 1963–1964; held at Rockefeller Museum, Jerusalem (papyri) and Israel Antiquities Authority (bullae)
**Approximate size:** ~18 partially legible Aramaic legal papyri; ~128 clay bullae with seal impressions; documents belong to a Samarian family archive
**Document types:** Property sale contracts (notably slave sales), land conveyances, legal oaths; the community appears to have fled Samaria during Alexander's destruction of the city (332 BCE), carrying their family archive into the cave where they died
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — published in DJD XXVIII (Gropp 2001) with English text and commentary; Dušek (2007, Brill) provides additional analysis; all legible texts are translated
**Digital availability:** Minimally available digitally — DJD XXVIII is the standard edition (academic library access only); no open-access digital portal; Comprehensive Aramaic Lexicon (CAL, https://cal.huc.edu) has bibliographic records for the Samaria papyri texts
**Primary digital home:** DJD XXVIII (academic); CAL bibliography at https://cal.huc.edu

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Property sale contracts**: slave and land sales with explicit prices — reference-price data for a Samarian community
- Named buyers and sellers: individuals traceable within the archive
- Legal conveyance language with conditional clauses
- The archive ends abruptly with the destruction of Samaria (332 BCE) — a crisis-period snapshot of property transactions just before political catastrophe
- Cross-legal context: Persian-period Aramaic law applied to a Samaritan community

**Assessment:** A small but well-defined family archive covering four decades of property transactions in the last years of Persian rule in Samaria. The slave and land sale prices provide reference-point data for a specific community. The dramatic end of the archive (community flight from Alexander's attack) creates a unique crisis-period snapshot. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** due to small size; the property price data and the crisis context are the most behavioral-economically interesting features.

### 3.11 Nabataean Papyri (c. 1st century BCE – 2nd century CE)

**Abbreviations:** WDSP (Babatha documents); IOSPE; PAT (Palmyrene Aramaic Texts, by analogy)
**Date range:** c. 50 BCE – 130 CE
**Language and script:** Nabataean Aramaic script; papyrus and leather
**Provenance:** Primarily the Cave of Letters (Nahal Hever), Judean Desert (as part of the Babatha archive, section 3.5); also a small number from Petra region and Wadi Murabba'at
**Approximate size:** ~23 Nabataean papyri known from all sources (per Yardeni's 2004 survey, which counted ~15 published items); several are Nabataean signatures on Greek documents; the Babatha archive contains 6 Nabataean documents among its 35 total
**Document types:** Legal contracts (property conveyances, marriage-related documents); the Babatha Nabataean documents include a registration of property and a loan document; a handful of additional contracts from other provenances
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — Yardeni's editions provide standard translations; Nabataean texts in the Babatha archive translated in JDS volumes; DiCoNab database (https://diconab.huma-num.fr/) provides updated bibliography and some editions
**Digital availability:** Partially available — DiCoNab (Digital Corpus of Nabataean and Developing Arabic, https://diconab.huma-num.fr/) is the primary digital resource for Nabataean epigraphy, containing >900 bibliographic references and a relational database of inscriptions; however, the papyri themselves are published primarily in print editions
**Primary digital home:** https://diconab.huma-num.fr/ (DiCoNab)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Property registration and conveyance with explicit asset valuations
- Loan documents with terms (Babatha's Nabataean loan)
- The Nabataean script represents the commercial culture of Petra — a major caravan trading hub — but surviving papyri are too few to constitute a commercial archive
- Named individuals in legal contexts

**Assessment:** The Nabataean papyrus corpus is tiny — ~23 items, of which roughly half are significant enough for detailed study. The Nabataeans were a major pre-Islamic trading civilization, but their merchant correspondence (if it existed in written form) has not survived in papyrus. The documents that survive are almost exclusively legal in character. Most behavioral-economics-relevant content from this community is covered under the Babatha archive (section 3.5). **Low behavioral signal** as a standalone corpus; primarily documented here for completeness and to note the DiCoNab digital resource.

### 3.12 Murabba'at and Other Judean Desert Aramaic Documents (1st–2nd century CE)

**Abbreviations:** Mur.; DJD II (Discoveries in the Judaean Desert, vol. 2); P.Mur.
**Date range:** Primarily 1st–2nd century CE (with some pre-CE material)
**Language and script:** Aramaic, Hebrew, Greek, Nabataean; papyrus and leather
**Provenance:** Wadi Murabba'at, Judean Desert; discovered 1951–1952 by Bedouin; excavated by Roland de Vaux (École Biblique); also related finds from Nahal Se'elim, Nahal Mishmar, and other caves
**Approximate size:** DJD II covers ~177 documents from Murabba'at; Bar Kokhba-period documents (partially overlapping with section 3.6) add further material; multiple Aramaic legal contracts among these
**Document types:** Marriage contracts (ketubot), bills of divorce (gets), property deeds, sale contracts, acknowledgments of debt, letters (including several from Bar Kokhba, overlap with section 3.6)
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — DJD II (Benoit, Milik, de Vaux, 1961) provides standard edition; subsequent specialized studies; Hebrew and Aramaic legal documents well studied
**Digital availability:** Minimally available — DJD II is the standard edition (academic access); no open-access digital portal; Comprehensive Aramaic Lexicon (CAL) has bibliographic entries; some documents appear in the Israel Antiquities Authority (IAA) database
**Primary digital home:** Academic editions (DJD II); CAL bibliography (https://cal.huc.edu)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Marriage contracts: explicit monetary terms (dowry, bride-price, alimony on divorce) — reference-amount data for a Judean community
- Bills of divorce with property settlement terms
- Sale contracts and debt acknowledgments: price and interest data
- Named individuals in a defined community (Judean desert settlements, 1st–2nd century CE)
- The crisis context (Bar Kokhba revolt) creates extreme decision-making conditions in the latest documents

**Assessment:** The Murabba'at documents provide legal and financial records for a Judean community bridging the first and second centuries CE. The marriage contracts and sale documents contain reference-price data. Taken together with the Babatha archive (3.5) and Bar Kokhba letters (3.6), these texts constitute a cluster of Judean Desert documentary evidence for economic behavior in Roman-period Judea. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal**; most useful in combination with other Judean Desert corpora.

### 3.13 Palmyrene Texts and the Palmyra Tariff (c. 1st–3rd century CE)

**Abbreviations:** PAT (Palmyrene Aramaic Texts, Hillers & Cussini 1996); CIS II 3913 (Palmyra Tariff); WPAIP (Wisconsin Palmyrene Aramaic Inscription Project)
**Date range:** Palmyrene epigraphy broadly c. 1st century BCE – 272 CE (city destroyed); the Tariff specifically dated to 137 CE
**Language and script:** Palmyrene Aramaic (a distinct dialect); bilingual Greek/Aramaic in the Tariff and many major inscriptions
**Provenance:** Palmyra (Tadmor, modern Syria); major corpus of inscriptions from the city's temples, tombs, and public spaces; the Tariff stele is now at the State Hermitage Museum, St. Petersburg
**Approximate size:** PAT (1996) collected the corpus known before 1996 — several thousand short inscriptions; the Wisconsin Palmyrene Aramaic Inscription Project (WPAIP) is re-collating the full corpus with new photographic documentation; the Tariff itself is a single four-column stele (~400 lines of bilingual text)
**Document types:** Funerary and votive inscriptions (majority); honorary and public inscriptions; the Tariff is a comprehensive municipal tax regulation governing goods traded through Palmyra; tessera (token) inscriptions for banquets and trade
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — major inscriptions translated in PAT; the Tariff translated in multiple editions (Matthews 1984 critical edition; Yon 2002 French; Speidel and colleagues); all key Palmyrene economic texts translated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — WPAIP (University of Wisconsin-Madison Libraries, https://search.library.wisc.edu/digital/AWPAIPColl) provides photographic records and digital editions; the Tariff is available in full in academic publications and partially online via the Hermitage Museum; PHI database has some Palmyrene texts
**Primary digital home:** https://search.library.wisc.edu/digital/AWPAIPColl (WPAIP); Hermitage Museum online collection for the Tariff

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Palmyra Tariff (CIS II 3913)**: one of the most detailed ancient price lists — 137 CE municipal tariff specifying taxes on goods entering and leaving Palmyra (purple-dyed textiles, myrrh, oil, dried figs, bronzes, pine nuts, slaves, prostitutes, and many others); explicit tax rates per unit and per quality grade
- The Tariff's bilingual format (Greek and Aramaic) enabled enforcement across a multilingual trading population
- Tesserae (banquet tokens): implied fixed-price exchange instruments circulating in the caravan trade
- Funerary banquet inscriptions: community social conventions around food and hospitality expenditure
- The Tariff is the longest known Canaanite-family inscription; it documents price schedules for a major Silk Road entrepôt
- Honorary inscriptions for caravan leaders: name wealthy traders and their social capital (reputation-based credit system)

**Assessment:** The Palmyra Tariff is one of the most behaviorally significant ancient documents for price-reference analysis. It provides a comprehensive, official, and dated schedule of trade taxes for a major caravan city at a precise moment (137 CE) — establishing exact reference prices for a wide range of goods. The honorary inscriptions for caravan leaders document reputation and social capital in ways relevant to behavioral economics of credit and trust. **Medium-high behavioral signal** specifically for the Tariff; **medium** for the broader epigraphic corpus. This corpus is under-exploited in ancient economic behavioral research.

### 3.14 Aramaic Papyri from Bactria — Khalili Collection (c. 353–324 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** KAD (Khalili Aramaic Documents); Naveh/Shaked 2012
**Date range:** c. 353–324 BCE (late Achaemenid through early Alexander period)
**Language and script:** Official Imperial Aramaic; ink on leather (30 items) and wooden tally sticks (18 items)
**Provenance:** Provenience unknown; attributed by editors to Balkh (ancient Bactra), capital of the Achaemenid satrapy of Bactria (modern Afghanistan); acquired through the antiquities market; Khalili Collections, London
**Approximate size:** 48 documents total: 30 on leather, 18 wooden tally sticks — the first ancient tally sticks (as a system of bookkeeping) found anywhere in the ancient Near East
**Document types:** Administrative letters and accounts connected with the court of Bagavant (governor of Bactria under satrap Akhvamazda); supply and commodity distribution records; tally sticks recording numerical transactions
**Translation status:** Fully translated — Naveh and Shaked (2012), Aramaic Documents from Ancient Bactria, Khalili Family Trust; complete edition with transliteration, translation, and commentary
**Digital availability:** Minimally available — the 2012 Naveh/Shaked publication is the standard edition (not freely online); the Khalili Collections website (https://www.khalilicollections.org/all-collections/aramaic-documents/) provides summary information; no open-access digital text portal
**Primary digital home:** https://www.khalilicollections.org/all-collections/aramaic-documents/ (summary); academic edition (Naveh/Shaked 2012)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative letters: supply requisitions and reporting within a satrapal court at the eastern edge of the Achaemenid Empire
- Tally sticks: numerical bookkeeping system for tracking transactions — the physical accounting instruments themselves survive
- Named officials (Bagavant and Akhvamazda) across multiple documents: repeated-agent data at the satrapal level
- Commodity distribution and allocation records
- The documents span the end of the Achaemenid period — administrative behavior in the transition to Alexander's conquest

**Assessment:** The Khalili Bactrian documents are the only substantial Aramaic administrative archive from the eastern extremity of the Achaemenid Empire. The tally sticks are a unique find with no parallel in the Near Eastern record — they document a physical bookkeeping system previously unknown. The administrative letters provide supply-chain and allocation data from a remote satrapal capital. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** for standard behavioral analysis (small size, administrative only); **high historical significance** for establishing that Achaemenid administrative practices reached Bactria in the same documentary form as in Egypt and Anatolia.

## 4. Greek Corpora

### 4.1 Linear B Tablets — Knossos, Pylos, Mycenae (c. 1450–1200 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** KN (Knossos); PY (Pylos); MY (Mycenae); TH (Thebes); DĀMOS
**Date range:** c. 1450–1200 BCE (Late Bronze Age)
**Language and script:** Mycenaean Greek; Linear B syllabic script on clay tablets
**Provenance:** Knossos (Crete): ~4,000 tablets; Pylos (Peloponnese): ~1,000+ tablets; Mycenae, Tiryns, Thebes, Chania: smaller collections
**Approximate size:** ~6,000 tablets total; Knossos ~4,000; Pylos ~1,000+; scribal hands identified: 45 at Pylos, 66 at Knossos
**Document types:** Palace administrative records exclusively — livestock counts, grain allocations, land tenure records, tribute lists, personnel rosters, equipment inventories; no letters, no private documents
**Translation status:** Fully translated (since Ventris's 1952 decipherment) — standard editions by Ventris/Chadwick (1973), Hooker, Killen; DĀMOS database (Oslo) provides online editions
**Digital availability:** Partially available — DĀMOS (https://damos.hum.uio.no/) provides online corpus; Pylos digitization project (Nakassis/Pluta) in progress; some 3D models
**Primary digital home:** https://damos.hum.uio.no/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Land tenure records**: land allocations and "lease-holders" — institutional reference quantities
- **Personnel and ration records**: allocations by rank and trade group
- **Livestock counts**: systematic animal inventories (tablets destroyed just before Bronze Age collapse — potential pre-crisis snapshot)
- **Named scribes**: 45 identified at Pylos — enables scribe-by-scribe comparison of recording practices
- No price data in market sense (command economy)
- No letters or individual decision-making records

**Assessment:** A closed palace administrative corpus with no market transactions or individual correspondence. The Linear B tablets document a redistributive command economy; there are no prices and no named traders making decisions under uncertainty. Useful for establishing palace resource allocation benchmarks. **Low behavioral signal** for bias detection; primarily valuable as a baseline for Bronze Age Mediterranean institutional economics.

### 4.2 Classical Greek Inscriptions (c. 500–300 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** IG (Inscriptiones Graecae); SEG (Supplementum Epigraphicum Graecum); PHI
**Date range:** c. 500–300 BCE (broadly; inscriptions span archaic through Hellenistic)
**Language and script:** Ancient Greek; stone inscription
**Provenance:** Athens and Attica (primary); also all Aegean islands, Asia Minor, Greece, Magna Graecia
**Approximate size:** ~210,000 inscriptions searchable via PHI Greek Inscriptions database
**Document types:** Honorific decrees, financial accounts (Parthenon building accounts), law codes, temple inventories, proxeny decrees, manumission records, casualty lists, commercial regulations
**Translation status:** Partially translated — IG series in German; Attic Inscriptions Online (AIO) provides English translations for Attic inscriptions; PHI is mostly transliteration without translation
**Digital availability:** Freely available — PHI Greek Inscriptions (https://inscriptions.packhum.org/); AIO (https://www.atticinscriptions.com/)
**Primary digital home:** https://inscriptions.packhum.org/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Athenian financial accounts** (IG I³ 436–451, Parthenon accounts): multi-year building expenditure records with price data for labor and materials — price-series potential
- **Temple inventories** (Delian, Athenian): commodity holdings tracked over years — reference stock levels
- **Commercial regulations**: price-control inscriptions from various cities
- **Manumission records**: freedom prices for slaves — asset valuation reference points
- **Maritime loan inscriptions** (bottomry loans): high-risk lending instruments with explicit risk premium structures
- Named individuals: architects, contractors, treasurers recurring across accounts

**Assessment:** A large but heterogeneous corpus. The financial accounts and maritime loan records are the most behaviorally relevant. The maritime bottomry loans are particularly interesting as explicit high-risk credit instruments where lenders and borrowers negotiated terms under uncertainty. **Medium behavioral signal**; most valuable for maritime loan analysis and Athenian financial management data.

### 4.4 Greek and Demotic Ostraca from Roman Egypt (c. 300 BCE – 400 CE)

**Abbreviations:** O.Petr. (Petrie Museum ostraca); BGU (Berliner Griechische Urkunden, for ostraca volumes); O.Mich. (Michigan ostraca); O.Wilck. (Wilcken ostraca)
**Date range:** c. 300 BCE – 400 CE (Ptolemaic through Roman period)
**Language and script:** Greek (primary), Demotic, bilingual Greek-Demotic; inscribed on pottery sherds (ostraca)
**Provenance:** Throughout Egypt — Thebes (Luxor), Elephantine, Fayum, Oxyrhynchus, and other sites; major collections at the British Museum (Petrie), Berlin Staatliche Museen, Michigan, Columbia, and Oriental Institute (Chicago)
**Approximate size:** Tens of thousands of ostraca in total across all collections; Berlin holds one of the largest single collections (~15,000 items); Columbia has ~3,600 ostraca; the Papyrus Portal (Germany) provides access to several thousand digitized ostraca from Berlin, Leipzig, Heidelberg, and Cologne collections
**Document types:** Tax receipts (the overwhelming majority — ostraka were the standard medium for tax payment receipts in Ptolemaic and Roman Egypt); short private and administrative notes; labels; short letters; grain and commodity receipts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — the standard reference is Wilcken's Griechische Ostraka (1899); modern critical editions for selected collections (O.Petr., O.Mich. series); tax receipt formulas are well understood; most ostraca are short and formulaic
**Digital availability:** Partially available — papyri.info integrates many ostraca alongside papyri; Papyrus Portal (https://papyrusportal.de) provides open access to German collections including Berlin; APIS (Advanced Papyrological Information System) at papyri.info covers multiple collections; Oriental Institute ISAC Demotic Ostraca project (https://isac.uchicago.edu/research/projects/isac-museum-demotic-ostraca-online) provides free digital access to Chicago's Demotic ostraca
**Primary digital home:** https://papyri.info (ostraca integrated with papyri); https://papyrusportal.de (German collections); https://isac.uchicago.edu (Demotic ostraca)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Tax receipts**: ostraca document annual tax payments by named individuals — enabling multi-year tracking of individual taxpayers and their payment behavior
- **Commodity receipts**: grain and other commodity receipts establish reference amounts for standard transactions
- **Named recurring agents**: individual taxpayers appear across multiple tax season receipts — repeated-agent data at the village level
- **Short private notes**: some ostraca document debts, loans, and complaints in abbreviated form
- The formulaic nature of tax receipts enables large-scale quantitative analysis of compliance patterns across decades

**Assessment:** Greek ostraca are distinct from papyri and deserve separate documentation. The tax receipt corpus is particularly valuable because it creates a systematic, recurring-agent dataset: the same individuals appear as taxpayers year after year, enabling analysis of payment patterns over time. While each individual receipt contains minimal information, the aggregate corpus across multiple tax seasons constitutes one of the largest repeated-transaction datasets in antiquity. **Medium behavioral signal** per document; **medium-high potential** for large-scale quantitative analysis of tax compliance and reference-point behavior.

### 4.5 Linear A Tablets (c. 1800–1450 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** GORILA (Recueil des inscriptions en Linéaire A, Godart & Olivier); CHIC (Corpus Hieroglyphicarum Inscriptionum Cretae); SigLA (Signs of Linear A)
**Date range:** c. 1800–1450 BCE (Middle Minoan IB through Late Minoan IA; Minoan palatial period)
**Language and script:** Linear A; syllabic script of unknown underlying language (Minoan — almost certainly not Indo-European); inscribed on clay tablets, stone vessels, and other media
**Provenance:** Primarily Crete (Hagia Triada most important archive: ~150 tablets; also Zakros, Phaistos, Knossos, Chania, Akrotiri on Thera, and sites in the Aegean); small quantities at non-Cretan sites suggest Minoan commercial reach
**Approximate size:** ~1,427 inscribed specimens; approximately 7,400 total signs; ~300 clay tablets (the administrative core); the remaining inscriptions are shorter votive, libation-table, and object labels
**Document types:** Administrative tablets (commodity accounts, ration records, personnel lists — identifiable by context and numerical notation); votive inscriptions on libation tables and stone vessels; short labels; the administrative tablets constitute roughly 60% of the corpus
**Translation status:** Not translated — the script is undeciphered; the underlying language is unknown; numerical portions and some logograms (for commodities) are understood by analogy with Linear B; the economic content of tablets is inferred from structural parallels with Linear B administrative texts
**Digital availability:** Partially available — SigLA (https://sigla.phis.me) is an open-access palaeographical database of Linear A signs; GORILA is the standard corpus (print); a Linear A digital corpus project has been undertaken (Aurora 2015 and related work); CDLI has some Linear A records
**Primary digital home:** https://sigla.phis.me (SigLA palaeographical database); GORILA print corpus

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative tablets: commodity accounts with quantities (inferred by logogram analysis) — potential ration and redistribution data
- Personnel lists: workforce allocation records (by analogy with Linear B)
- Numeric notation understood: proportional relationships and ratios can be extracted
- No named agents recoverable (script undeciphered; personal names not identifiable with certainty)
- The administrative structure is identifiable even without decipherment — a palace redistribution economy parallel to Linear B

**Assessment:** Linear A is documented here for completeness. The administrative tablets almost certainly contain ration and commodity distribution data analogous to Linear B, based on structural and logogram analysis. However, the undeciphered state of the script means that no economic quantities, agent names, or conditional clauses can be verified with confidence. The corpus is too small (~300 administrative tablets) and too opaque to support behavioral analysis directly. **No usable behavioral signal** in current state; documented as a potential resource contingent on future decipherment progress.

### 4.6 Cypro-Minoan Script Documents (c. 1550–1050 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** CM (Cypro-Minoan); DĀMOS (for related Mycenaean context); Ferrara (2012) corpus
**Date range:** c. 1550–1050 BCE (Late Bronze Age Cyprus)
**Language and script:** Cypro-Minoan; a syllabic script partially derived from Linear A; underlying language(s) unknown; may represent more than one language
**Provenance:** Cyprus (Enkomi primary archive; also Ugarit, Tiryns, Ras Shamra); clay tablets, clay balls, and inscribed bronze objects
**Approximate size:** ~230 texts (Ferrara 2012 corpus); the largest set is from Enkomi (~109 tablets and objects)
**Document types:** Administrative tablets (commodity accounts, comparable in structure to Linear B); a small number of inscribed objects that may be labels or tallies
**Translation status:** Partially deciphered — the script has been partially read (approximately 50–70 signs identified) but the underlying language remains unknown; economic functions of tablets are inferred from structure; full decipherment has not been achieved as of 2026
**Digital availability:** Minimally available — Ferrara (2012) is the standard corpus edition (print); some records in CDLI; no dedicated open-access digital portal
**Primary digital home:** Ferrara (2012) print corpus; CDLI (partial records)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Administrative tablets with commodity quantities (inferred from structural analysis)
- Possible trade-related documents given Cyprus's role as a major Bronze Age copper trading hub
- No named agents, no prices, no complaint genre recoverable in current decipherment state

**Assessment:** Documented for completeness. Cypro-Minoan almost certainly contains administrative economic records — Cyprus was the primary copper source for the Late Bronze Age Mediterranean and had an active palace economy. However, the partial decipherment and small corpus size preclude any behavioral analysis. **No usable behavioral signal** in current state; may become relevant if decipherment advances.

## 5. Roman Corpora

### 5.1 Pompeii Wax Tablets: Sulpicii and Iucundii Archives (1st century CE)

**Abbreviations (Sulpicii):** TPSulp (Tabulae Pompeianae Sulpiciorum); Camodeca edition
**Abbreviations (Iucundii):** CIL IV Suppl. (Corpus Inscriptionum Latinarum); ILS; Iucundus Tablets
**Date range:** Sulpicii: 26–61 CE; Iucundii: c. 52–62 CE
**Language and script:** Latin; wax on wooden tablets (triptychs, diptychs)
**Provenance:** Sulpicii: Murecine (near Puteoli/Pozzuoli), Campania, excavated 1959; Iucundii: House V.1.26, Pompeii, found 1875
**Approximate size:** Sulpicii: 127+ texts (>120 legible); Iucundii: 153 partially legible documents
**Document types:**
- Sulpicii: Private legal practice records — loans, sureties, deposits, maritime commerce documents; the largest single body of evidence for private Roman legal practice
- Iucundii: Banker (coactor argentarius) records — receipts for property sales, slave sales, rent collections, tax auctions, loans
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — Camodeca edition of Sulpicii (1999) with Italian commentary; Wolf (2010) English/German; Iucundii published in CIL with Latin text; English translations in specialized studies
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Trismegistos database (https://www.trismegistos.org) has records; individual texts in academic publications; no single free online corpus
**Primary digital home:** https://www.trismegistos.org (Sulpicii arch_id 230); academic repositories

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Loan documents**: both archives saturated with credit instruments at documented interest rates
- **Maritime commerce (Sulpicii)**: high-risk nautical loan records with risk premium structures
- **Tax and auction records (Iucundii)**: tax collection receipts that document realized vs. expected revenue flows
- **Named recurring agents**: Caius Sulpicius Faustus and family members appear across all Sulpicii documents; Iucundus's counterparties are named
- **Property and slave sales**: market prices documented for specific assets
- **Maritime loan risk structure**: explicit risk allocation between lender and borrower
- **Guarantor networks**: multiple named sureties per loan — social-network risk-sharing

**Assessment:** The Sulpicii archive is the most important source for private Roman commercial law in practice. The maritime loan documents are especially valuable for risk preference analysis — they represent formal, explicit risk instruments where both parties negotiated terms under stated uncertainty about voyage outcomes. **High behavioral relevance** for risk premium analysis, credit market behavior, and repeated-agent networks.

### 5.2 Vindolanda Tablets (c. 85–130 CE)

**Abbreviations:** TV (Tabulae Vindolandenses); Tab. Vindol.
**Date range:** c. 85–130 CE (principally c. 97–105 CE)
**Language and script:** Latin; ink on thin wooden leaves
**Provenance:** Vindolanda fort (near Hadrian's Wall), Northumberland, England; excavated since 1970; ongoing discoveries (>1,700 tablets to date)
**Approximate size:** >1,700 tablets; principal published corpus ~600 tablets in TV volumes I–IV
**Document types:** Military correspondence (letters from officers and their families), strength reports (renuntia), requests for supplies, accounts, leave requests, birthday invitation
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — TV I–IV (Bowman and Thomas) with full Latin text and English translation; online edition at romaninscriptionsofbritain.org
**Digital availability:** Freely available — Roman Inscriptions of Britain (https://romaninscriptionsofbritain.org/tabvindol/) provides complete published corpus with images, transcription, and translation
**Primary digital home:** https://romaninscriptionsofbritain.org/tabvindol/

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Supply requisition letters**: requests for goods with expected and actual supply levels
- **Military accounts**: commodity prices and quantities (cereals, hides, leather, pigs, wine, oil)
- **Private letters**: requests from officers' families with explicit reference to needs and expectations
- **Strength reports**: troop numbers as baseline reference levels
- **Leave requests**: conditional approval documents
- Named recurring officers: Flavius Cerialis (prefect) and his wife Sulpicia Lepidina prominent
- The Vindolanda accounts document genuine commodity prices in northern Britain

**Assessment:** The most vivid corpus of personal Latin correspondence from the ancient world. The mix of military supply accounts, personal letters, and commodity price data makes this an interesting behavioral corpus. The accounts provide price data; the letters provide evidence of expectation formation and complaint behavior. **Medium behavioral signal** due to primarily military/administrative focus; valuable for commodity price series and complaint-letter analysis.

### 5.3 Roman Dacia Wax Tablets — Alburnus Maior (131–167 CE)

**Abbreviations:** CIL III suppl. (TabCerD); Tab.Alb.
**Date range:** 131–167 CE
**Language and script:** Latin; wax on wooden tablets
**Provenance:** Alburnus Maior (modern Roșia Montană / Verespatak), Alba County, Romania; found in gold mine galleries 1786–1855
**Approximate size:** ~53 tablets (of which ~40 from main find lots); most record legal transactions
**Document types:** Labor contracts (locatio operarum), sales contracts, loan documents, mining partnership agreements (societates), legal oaths
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — published in CIL III supplementa; modern critical editions by Rodger (labor contracts), Camodeca; individual texts translated in specialized studies
**Digital availability:** Partially available — EAGLE epigraphic network has records; individual tablets being digitized with H-RTI technology
**Primary digital home:** https://www.eagle-network.eu; digitized specimens via ResearchGate

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Labor contracts**: explicit wage terms with daily rates and performance conditions — reference wage analysis
- **Mining partnership contracts**: risk-sharing agreements with explicit division of uncertain mining yields
- **Loan documents**: interest-bearing loans in a frontier mining community
- **Sales contracts**: market prices for specific goods in a defined community
- Named individuals in a defined geographic community (the Alburnus Maior mining settlement)
- **Risk structure**: mining contracts explicitly acknowledge uncertain yields — risk premium documentation
- Cross-cultural community: Dalmatian miners, Roman officials, local Dacians

**Assessment:** A small but thematically rich corpus. The mining partnership contracts are among the most explicit ancient documents for risk-sharing under uncertainty — parties negotiated explicit divisions of unknown future ore yields. Labor contracts provide reference wage data. The defined community enables network analysis. **High behavioral signal per document** despite tiny corpus size; particularly valuable for risk-sharing and reference-wage analysis.

### 5.4 Dura-Europos Documents (c. 100–256 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Dura (The Excavations at Dura-Europos: The Parchments and Papyri); C.Dura (cuneiform texts)
**Date range:** c. 100–256 CE (city destroyed by Sasanians 256 CE)
**Language and script:** Greek (majority); Latin (military documents); Aramaic; Palmyrene; Middle Persian (Parthian); a few in Syriac and Pahlavi
**Provenance:** Dura-Europos (Salihiyeh), Euphrates frontier, eastern Syria; excavated by Yale University and the French Academy of Inscriptions and Letters 1928–1937; materials primarily at Yale University Art Gallery
**Approximate size:** ~264 texts total in the standard P.Dura edition (Welles, Fink, Gilliam 1959): ~147 Latin (mainly military), ~52 Greek, smaller numbers in other languages; additional parchments and inscriptions bring the total higher; ~12,000 artifacts in total from the site at Yale
**Document types:** Military unit rosters, strength reports (pridianum), pay records and account books; commercial contracts (sale, loan, lease); parchment deeds (Greek and Aramaic); legal and civil documents; a synagogue fresco inscription; early church records
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — P.Dura (1959) provides full edition with English translations; Latin military papyri re-edited by James (2021, Cambridge) with updated translations; Greek contracts well translated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — the Yale Dura-Europos archive (https://duraeuropos.artgallery.yale.edu/) provides digital access to site photographs and some artifact records; papyri.info includes some P.Dura texts; Duke Databank has some Greek texts from Dura; the P.Dura Latin military papyri are included in papyri.info
**Primary digital home:** https://duraeuropos.artgallery.yale.edu/ (Yale archive); papyri.info (Greek texts)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Military pay records**: unit account books document soldier pay, deductions, and savings — potentially the most detailed ancient military personal finance records
- **Military strength reports (pridianum)**: expected vs. actual troop strengths — expectation-outcome comparison at the unit level
- **Commercial contracts**: private sale, loan, and lease documents from the civilian community in a frontier garrison town
- Named recurring individuals in military records (soldiers serving across multiple documents)
- **Multilingual economic interaction**: Greek, Aramaic, Latin, and Parthian speakers all conducting documented transactions in the same city
- A unique frontier economy: Roman garrison, Parthian/Sasanian neighboring territory, and caravan trade all intersecting

**Assessment:** Dura-Europos is particularly valuable as a document of economic behavior at the interface of Roman, Parthian, and Syrian cultural zones. The military pay and account records are among the most detailed Roman financial records for ordinary soldiers. The commercial contracts show private economic behavior in a frontier garrison context. **Medium behavioral signal** overall; the military financial records are **medium-high** for reference-wage analysis, and the multilingual commercial contracts are useful for cross-cultural transaction behavior.

### 5.5 Herculaneum Papyri (before 79 CE)

**Abbreviations:** PHerc. (Papyri Herculanenses); Hercul.
**Date range:** Pre-79 CE (carbonized in the Vesuvius eruption); the library was assembled probably in the 1st century BCE – 1st century CE
**Language and script:** Greek (majority); Latin (minority, including some financial texts)
**Provenance:** Villa of the Papyri, Herculaneum (modern Ercolano), Campania, Italy; discovered in Bourbon-era excavations 1752–1754; ~1,800 carbonized scrolls recovered; now primarily at the National Library in Naples (Biblioteca Nazionale di Napoli)
**Approximate size:** ~1,800 papyri (scrolls and fragments); roughly 600 unrolled and read before modern imaging technology; ~600 additional scrolls remain unread (as of 2025); text content dominated by Epicurean philosophy (Philodemus)
**Document types:** Primarily philosophical texts (Epicurean; primarily Philodemus of Gadara); some Latin legal and financial texts; a small number of texts on other subjects; the financial content is limited
**Translation status:** Substantially translated for the philosophical texts; the financial and non-philosophical minority texts partially translated; substantial new content being recovered via the Vesuvius Challenge (2023–2025) using CT scanning and AI-assisted ink detection
**Digital availability:** Substantially available — the Vesuvius Challenge (https://scrollprize.org/) has made high-resolution CT scan data for 4 complete scrolls and numerous fragments openly available; multispectral imaging by Brigham Young University since 1999; the 2023 Grand Prize awarded for successfully reading ~5% of one scroll; 2024 target: 90% of Scrolls 1–4
**Primary digital home:** https://scrollprize.org/ (Vesuvius Challenge, CT data and fragment images); Biblioteca Nazionale di Napoli for physical access

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Epicurean philosophical content: discussions of rational choice, pleasure, and pain — conceptually relevant to behavioral economics but not documentary evidence of actual economic decisions
- The recovered Latin financial texts (minor): some account-book content
- Risk and uncertainty discussions in philosophical texts (not documentary)
- The recovery process itself is ongoing — new financial content may emerge from the unread scrolls as the Vesuvius Challenge proceeds

**Assessment:** The Herculaneum library is primarily philosophical rather than documentary-economic. The Epicurean texts discuss rational decision-making at a theoretical level, which is contextually relevant but not direct evidence of behavioral bias. The small number of Latin financial texts within the corpus may yield additional data as new scrolls are read via the Vesuvius Challenge. As of April 2026, the financial content is limited and incompletely recovered. **Low behavioral signal** in current state; monitored for new discoveries from the Vesuvius Challenge digitization effort.

### 5.6 Nessana Papyri (c. 500–700 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Ness.; Nessana
**Date range:** c. 505–700 CE (Byzantine and early Islamic periods)
**Language and script:** Greek (majority of administrative and legal documents); Latin (a few literary texts); early Arabic (several documents from post-conquest period, including one dated to the 680s CE, P.Ness. 77)
**Provenance:** Nessana (ancient Nessana / Auja el-Hafir), Negev desert, Israel; excavated by H. Dunscombe Colt 1935–1937; papyri found in one of the village's churches; held at the Pierpont Morgan Library, New York (now Morgan Library)
**Approximate size:** ~195 documents and ~13 literary texts; 96 judged by the editor (Kraemer 1958) to be important enough for full publication; the rest are brief or fragmentary
**Document types:** Land registers, contracts (sale, loan, lease), family correspondence, military requisitions, tax documents, literary texts (including fragments of the Aeneid and a Greek-Latin glossary), early Arabic administrative documents
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — Kraemer (1958), "Non-Literary Papyri," Excavations at Nessana vol. 3, published 96 key texts with full English translations; digitization project by ISAW (Institute for the Study of the Ancient World, NYU) produced additional access
**Digital availability:** Partially available — ISAW (Institute for the Study of the Ancient World) digitized the collection with Pierpont Morgan Library cooperation; papyri.info has some Nessana texts; the Arabic papyri (including P.Ness. 77, the earliest papyrus mentioning the dhimma) are accessible in Islamic-awareness.org scholarly discussions
**Primary digital home:** papyri.info (search: Nessana); ISAW digital resources (https://isaw.nyu.edu)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Land registers**: multi-year property records for a defined village community — reference-asset tracking
- **Loan and sale contracts**: private economic transactions in a Byzantine desert village
- **Tax documents**: annual assessments enabling reference-point benchmarking
- **Family correspondence**: interpersonal financial matters, supply requests
- Named recurring individuals in a single defined community across ~200 years
- The transition from Byzantine to early Islamic administration is documented within the archive — institutional change with potential behavioral adaptation evidence
- Arabic documents from post-conquest period: economic continuity or disruption across the political transition

**Assessment:** The Nessana archive is one of the very few papyrus finds from outside Egypt, making it valuable for establishing whether Egyptian behavioral patterns were parochial or widespread. The village archive covers a single community across two centuries and two political systems (Byzantine and Islamic). The land and contract records provide reference-asset data. The transition documents are particularly interesting for studying institutional adaptation. **Medium behavioral signal**; particularly valuable for cross-regional comparison and institutional-change analysis.

### 5.7 Petra Papyri (c. 537–593 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Petra; Petra Pap.
**Date range:** c. 537–593 CE (Byzantine period, reign of Justinian through Maurice)
**Language and script:** Byzantine Greek; papyrus (charred)
**Provenance:** Petra, Jordan; discovered 1992 during ACOR excavation of the Petra Church (Byzantine); ~140 charred scrolls recovered from a bench; conserved and published by University of Helsinki and University of Michigan teams; held by the Petra Archaeological Museum under supervision of the Jordan Department of Antiquities
**Approximate size:** ~152 charred scrolls (many partially legible); 5 published volumes (P.Petra I–V, ACOR Publications, 1999–2018); text recovered corresponds to perhaps 75–100 legible documents
**Document types:** Property documents (land sales, land leases, property registration); family archive of a wealthy Petra landowner (Theodoros son of Obodianos) and his relatives; marriage documents; administrative records showing Byzantine provincial taxation; a few letters
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — P.Petra I–V provides Greek texts with full English translations and commentary; the five-volume series was completed in 2018
**Digital availability:** Minimally available digitally — ACOR (American Center of Oriental Research) publishes and holds the volumes (https://acorjordan.org/the-petra-papyri/); Trismegistos has records; no open-access digital text edition
**Primary digital home:** https://acorjordan.org/the-petra-papyri/ (ACOR publication information); Trismegistos (https://www.trismegistos.org) for metadata

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Land sale and lease contracts**: property transactions with explicit prices — reference-asset valuations for Byzantine Petra
- **Named family archive**: Theodoros and his relatives appear across multiple documents — multi-generational family estate management traceable
- **Taxation records**: Byzantine provincial tax documentation with property assessments
- **Marriage documents**: dowry and property settlement terms
- Property values in a documented community over ~56 years (537–593 CE)
- The Byzantine legal and fiscal framework is well understood, enabling the Petra data to be placed in comparative context

**Assessment:** The Petra papyri are the single most important documentary archive from Byzantine Jordan. The family archive structure (multiple documents relating to one wealthy family's estate) enables the kind of multi-document individual tracking that is most valuable for behavioral analysis. The land transaction prices provide reference-asset data for a dated community. **Medium-high behavioral signal** for the property and family archive documents; the multi-generational estate management context is particularly useful.

### 5.8 Ravenna Papyri (5th–7th century CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Ital. (Papiri Italiani); Tjäder (standard edition)
**Date range:** c. 445–665/666 CE (from the fall of the western Roman Empire through the Byzantine exarchate)
**Language and script:** Latin (cursive); some documents bilingual with Greek elements
**Provenance:** Ravenna, Italy (seat of the Western Roman court from 402, then Ostrogothic and Byzantine capital of Italy); now dispersed across European and North American libraries — Archivio Arcivescovile di Ravenna, Vatican Library, Paris (BnF), Vienna, Leiden, Princeton, Manchester, and others
**Approximate size:** ~60 documents in the core corpus (445 – mid-7th century); the Ravenna papyri constitute the single largest corpus of Latin papyri documents surviving from late antiquity
**Document types:** Ecclesiastical land administration documents — wills, donations to churches and monasteries, heritable leases of church lands, sales of church lands; legal charters; a handful of secular private documents
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — Tjäder's standard edition (Die nichtliterarischen lateinischen Papyri Italiens aus der Zeit 445–700, 2 vols., 1955–1982) provides critical texts with German commentary; English translations in specialized studies
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Trismegistos (https://www.trismegistos.org/collection/294) has records for the Ravenna Museo Arcivescovile collection; individual digitized documents at Manchester Digital Collections (https://www.digitalcollections.manchester.ac.uk) and Princeton University Library; no single comprehensive open-access text portal
**Primary digital home:** Trismegistos (https://www.trismegistos.org); individual institutional digitization efforts

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Church land transactions**: donations, leases, and sales with explicit valuations — reference-asset benchmarking for late antique Italy
- Testamentary documents: estate valuations and distributions — asset reference points at death
- Heritable lease records: long-term lease agreements with rent reference amounts
- Named ecclesiastical officials and private individuals across multiple transactions
- The corpus documents economic continuity across the fall of Rome, Odoacer's rule, Ostrogothic period, and Byzantine reconquest — institutional persistence under political disruption
- Latin legal and commercial formulas persist across all political transitions — status quo bias in legal format?

**Assessment:** The Ravenna papyri are the main evidence for private economic activity in post-Roman Italy. The church land transaction records are primarily ecclesiastical but contain genuine market prices for property. The continuity of legal formulas across multiple political regime changes is interesting for institutional persistence research. **Low-to-medium behavioral signal** for standard bias analysis; medium for institutional persistence and reference-price stability under political disruption.

### 5.9 Karanis Papyri (c. 1st–4th century CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Mich. (Michigan Papyri); O.Mich. (Michigan ostraca); APIS UM
**Date range:** c. 1st–4th century CE (primarily 2nd–3rd century CE)
**Language and script:** Greek (primary); some Demotic; a few Latin
**Provenance:** Karanis (modern Kom Aushim), Fayum, Egypt; excavated by University of Michigan 1924–1935; primary collection at Kelsey Museum of Archaeology (University of Michigan, Ann Arbor)
**Approximate size:** The University of Michigan holds >7,000 papyrus inventory numbers (many with multiple fragments, ~17,000 individual fragments total) from across Egypt, with Karanis being the primary excavated source; the Karanis-specific excavated material includes several thousand papyri and ostraca
**Document types:** Tax registers (annual grain tax, poll tax, money tax); private letters; contracts (sale, loan, lease); military documents; census declarations; petitions; granary accounts
**Translation status:** Substantially translated — P.Mich. series (University of Michigan Press) publishes editions with English translations; APIS UM (Advanced Papyrological Information System at Michigan) provides digital access; Michigan papyri are among the best-published American collections
**Digital availability:** Freely available — APIS UM (https://quod.lib.umich.edu/a/apis/) provides digitized images and transcriptions for many Karanis documents; papyri.info integrates P.Mich. texts; "Diversity in the Desert" digital exhibition (https://apps.lib.umich.edu/diversity-desert/) provides accessible presentation of economic life at Karanis
**Primary digital home:** https://quod.lib.umich.edu/a/apis/ (APIS UM); papyri.info (P.Mich. texts)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Village-level tax registers**: multi-year tax records for a single village community — the most complete economic dataset for an individual Roman Egyptian village, spanning ~300 years of habitation
- **Named recurring taxpayers**: individual residents of Karanis appear across multiple tax seasons — repeated-agent tracking possible
- **Private letters**: interpersonal financial correspondence from the same community
- **Loan and lease contracts**: credit instruments with interest rates from a single defined locality
- **Granary accounts**: commodity storage and distribution records enabling reference-stock analysis
- **Census declarations**: household composition and asset declarations — reference-asset benchmarking at household level
- The excavated context (documents found in situ in houses) allows some documents to be linked to specific households

**Assessment:** Karanis is unique among Roman Egyptian sites because the University of Michigan conducted systematic excavations that preserved the spatial context of document finds within individual houses. This means some documents can be linked to specific households, enabling a depth of community-level analysis not possible with market-acquired papyri. The 300-year span of occupation and the dense tax records make Karanis the most complete individual-village economic archive in antiquity. **High behavioral relevance** for community-level and individual repeated-agent analysis; under-exploited relative to its potential.

## 6. Other Corpora

### 6.1 Chinese Oracle Bones (Shang Dynasty, c. 1250–1050 BCE)

**Abbreviations:** OBI; OB; Jiaguwen (甲骨文); HJ (Heji, standard catalog)
**Date range:** c. 1250–1050 BCE (late Shang Dynasty, reigns of Wu Ding through Di Xin)
**Language and script:** Oracle bone script (ancestral Chinese logographic characters); inscribed on ox scapulae and turtle plastrons
**Provenance:** Yinxu (modern Anyang, Henan Province, China); most found in systematic excavations since 1928; ~130,000 inscriptions in China and 14 other countries
**Approximate size:** ~150,000 estimated inscriptions; ~130,000 in known collections; ~13,000 individual bones with inscriptions
**Document types:** Divination records — charges put to royal ancestors and spirits via pyromancy; topics include harvests, weather, military campaigns, hunts, health, ritual sacrifices, appointments
**Translation status:** Partially translated — ~1,200 characters deciphered with scholarly consensus; ~5,000 total characters identified; specialized translations of topic-grouped inscriptions in English, Japanese, German
**Digital availability:** Partially available — Yinxu Oracle Bone Inscription Digital Database (YOD, subscription-based); Jingyuan Digital Platform (free tools and search); Tencent Oracle Bone Corpus (3D models); Cambridge University Library digital holdings
**Primary digital home:** https://digitalorientalist.com/2022/09/23/cracking-the-oracle-bones/ (guide); YOD database (subscription)

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- **Forecast requests**: the oracle bone inscriptions are structured forecast queries — the diviner inscribes a charge ("Will there be rain this ten-day period?"), performs the ritual, and records the outcome of the crack
- **Paired charge-outcome records**: some bones record both the charge (prediction) and the verification (did it happen?) — a direct forecast-accuracy dataset
- **Repeated diviners**: named diviners appear across hundreds of inscriptions — enabling individual-level accuracy analysis
- **Decision framing**: charges posed in positive and negative form on the same bone ("We will attack X" vs. "We will not attack X") — explicit decision framing under uncertainty
- **Calibration evidence**: the repetition structure (same question on positive/negative) is a natural probe of decision-maker uncertainty
- **Agricultural and military forecasts**: domains with independent outcome verification possible from archaeology

**Assessment:** Assessed on merit, the Shang oracle bones are the non-Mesopotamian corpus most directly relevant to behavioral economics research on forecasting and overconfidence. Unlike any other ancient corpus, they record structured forecast queries with named forecasters, explicit binary framing, and some outcome verification. The paired positive/negative charge format is a natural within-bone calibration instrument. The translation difficulty (partially deciphered script) and the primarily subscription-based digital access are constraints. **High behavioral relevance** specifically for overconfidence and forecast calibration research; medium overall given access and translation barriers.

### 6.3 Additional Corpus: Tebtunis Papyri (c. 250 BCE – 300 CE)

**Abbreviations:** P.Tebt.; Tebtunis
**Date range:** c. 250 BCE – 300 CE
**Language and script:** Greek, Demotic, hieratic
**Provenance:** Tebtunis (modern Umm el-Breigat), Fayum, Egypt; excavated by Grenfell, Hunt, and Hogarth 1899–1900
**Approximate size:** ~30,000 fragments; ~3,000 published texts in P.Tebt. volumes
**Document types:** Tax rolls, land surveys, contracts, petitions, correspondence, temple records, literary texts
**Translation status:** Partially translated — P.Tebt. series (4 volumes) with English translations; many fragments untranslated
**Digital availability:** Partially available — papyri.info and APIS (UC Berkeley and other institutions) host many texts
**Primary digital home:** https://papyri.info; https://apis.lib.umich.edu

**Behavioral economics signals:**
- Tax rolls with year-by-year land assessments (reference-point benchmarking)
- Land survey data across multiple years
- Petitions (complaint documents)
- Loan and lease contracts

**Assessment:** A large fragmentary corpus with strong administrative and financial content. The tax rolls are useful for reference-price analysis. Medium behavioral signal; subsumed within the broader Roman Egypt papyri corpus (Section 2.8).

## Notes on Behavioral Economics Signal Rating

**Highest**: Direct forecast records with named agents and outcome data; multi-commodity price series spanning decades or centuries; explicit loss-framing language in complaint letters linked to recoverable reference points.

**High**: Named recurring agents trackable across multiple documents; loan/debt instruments with interest rates; price series of at least one commodity across 10+ years; private merchant letters expressing expectations.

**Medium–High**: Named agents and some price data, but incomplete tracking; partial price series; institutional records with reference allocation data; legal documents with embedded price information.

**Medium**: Institutional administrative records with quantities but limited price data; partial agent tracking; translated letters without consistent price references.

**Low–Medium**: Primarily administrative records with no individual decision-makers; no price data in market sense; institutional allocation records only.

**Low**: No prices, no named agents, no complaint genre, no letters; purely administrative accounts or military records.
